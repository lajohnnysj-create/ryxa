How to Price Your First Online Course

Ryxa 📅 April 2026 ⏱ 7 min read

You built the course. You recorded the videos, wrote the lessons, designed the modules. Now you're staring at the price field, and everything in your head is screaming to type "$19."

Don't.

Pricing is the single decision that will define whether your course business thrives or quietly dies. Get it wrong and you'll attract the wrong audience, burn out from volume, and wonder why you're making less than a part-time barista. Get it right and you build something sustainable.

The $0 Trap

Let's start with the most tempting mistake: making your course free. The logic sounds solid — "I'll give it away to build an audience, then charge later." But here's what actually happens.

Free courses have abysmal completion rates. People who pay nothing assign zero value. They download, bookmark, and never open it again. You end up with 500 "students" and zero testimonials because nobody finished the first module.

Worse, you've now trained your audience to expect free. When you launch your next course at $97, they're shocked. "Why would I pay when the first one was free?" You've undermined your own pricing power before you started.

Free doesn't build an audience. It builds a list of people who don't value your work.

There's one exception: a free course can work as a lead magnet — a short, high-value preview that funnels into a paid course. But the free course should be a taste, not the meal. Think 3-4 lessons, not 30.

Why $19 Is Worse Than $97

This is counterintuitive, but a $19 course often sells worse than a $97 course. Here's why.

At $19, you're competing with YouTube. The buyer thinks: "Is this really worth $19 when I can learn this for free on YouTube?" You've placed yourself in the commodity zone where every creator is interchangeable.

At $97, you've signaled something different. The buyer thinks: "This must be structured, comprehensive, and worth investing in." The price itself communicates value. It says this isn't a random collection of tips — it's a system, a framework, a transformation.

The psychology works in your favor too. Someone who pays $97 is committed. They show up, do the work, get results, and leave a testimonial. Someone who pays $19 treats it like an impulse buy and forgets about it by Thursday.

The Value Equation

Stop thinking about how many hours of content you have. Nobody cares that your course is 12 hours long. They care about the outcome.

Ask yourself: what is the result worth to the buyer? If your course teaches someone how to land their first freelance client, that client might be worth $2,000-5,000. Suddenly $197 for your course is an absurd bargain. If your course teaches someone how to grow their Instagram from 0 to 10K, what would they pay an agency for that? $500/month? Your $97 course is a steal.

Price based on the transformation, not the content. The content is the vehicle. The destination is what people are paying for.

Nobody pays for information. They pay for transformation. Price accordingly.

The Pricing Framework

Here's a practical framework that works for first-time course creators.

Mini-course (1-2 hours, 5-10 lessons): $27-47. This is a focused, specific skill. "How to Edit Reels Like a Pro" or "Email Marketing Basics for Creators." Quick win, low commitment.

Standard course (3-8 hours, 15-30 lessons): $97-197. This is a comprehensive system. "Build Your Brand from Scratch" or "Complete YouTube Growth Strategy." Multiple modules, clear progression, real transformation.

Premium course (8+ hours, community access, templates): $297-497. This is the full package. Includes everything in a standard course plus bonus materials, templates, community access, or coaching calls. Reserved for creators with established credibility.

If you're launching your first course, the $47-97 range is the sweet spot. High enough to signal value, low enough to reduce purchase anxiety. You can always raise the price later as you add content and collect testimonials.

The Launch Price Strategy

Here's a strategy that works well for first-time launches. Set your "real" price at $97, but offer an early-bird price of $47 for the first 50 students (or first 2 weeks). This creates urgency without devaluing your course — the discount is temporary and justified by being early.

After the early-bird window closes, move to full price. Now you have students, testimonials, and social proof to justify the higher number. Every future student sees the reviews and thinks "that's worth $97."

Never permanently discount. If your course is always "on sale," the sale price becomes the real price and nobody trusts the original number.

What About Free Lessons?

This is different from making the whole course free. Marking 1-2 lessons as "free preview" is smart — it lets potential buyers see your teaching style, the production quality, and the depth of content before committing. Think of it as trying on shoes before buying.

The best lesson to make free is the one that delivers immediate value but leaves them wanting the full system. Give them a quick win, then show them the bigger picture is behind the paywall.

Pricing Mistakes to Avoid

Don't price based on competitors. If every course in your niche is $29, that doesn't mean yours should be $29. It means everyone in your niche is underpricing. Be the premium option.

Don't apologize for your price. If you write "I know this might seem expensive, but..." you've already lost. Price with confidence or don't price at all.

Don't offer too many price tiers. One price, one product. Maybe a premium tier with extras. Three tiers confuse people. Confused people don't buy.

Don't give lifetime access for free updates. If you significantly update the course later, you can offer the updated version as a separate purchase or an upgrade. Lifetime free updates sounds generous but devalues your future work.

The Bottom Line

Your knowledge has value. The time you spent learning, failing, and figuring things out — that has value. The transformation you can provide to someone who is where you were two years ago — that has enormous value.

Don't let imposter syndrome set your prices. The market will tell you if you're too high — you can always adjust. But starting too low is a hole that's hard to climb out of.

Pick a number that makes you slightly uncomfortable. That's probably the right price.
