How to Price Your Online Course

Ryxa Team • April 29, 2026 • 9 min read

You've finished the course. The lessons are recorded, the workbooks are designed, the modules are organized. Then you hit the price field and freeze.

Type $19 and you'll regret it. Type $497 and you'll worry nobody buys. Type something in between and you'll wonder if you're leaving money on the table.

Pricing is the single decision that defines whether your course business works or quietly collapses. The good news is that there's a framework, and it's simpler than most pricing advice makes it out to be.

Why Most Course Creators Underprice

The default instinct for first-time course creators is to price low. The thinking sounds reasonable: "I'm new, my brand is small, I should keep prices accessible to attract buyers."

This is almost always the wrong move. Low pricing creates three serious problems.

It attracts the wrong customers. The audience willing to pay $19 is the most price-sensitive, demanding, refund-happy segment of the market. They expect handholding, complain the loudest, and rarely complete what they paid for. The audience that pays $197 takes their investment seriously, does the work, and leaves you testimonials that drive future sales.

It traps you in volume. At $19, you need 100 buyers to make $1,900. At $197, you need 10. Which is easier — finding 10 motivated buyers or 100 bargain hunters? Volume strategies require massive audiences and constant marketing. High-ticket strategies work with smaller, more engaged audiences.

It signals low quality. Buyers use price as a signal of value. A $19 course is assumed to be light, surface-level, "things I could find on YouTube." A $197 course is assumed to be a system, a framework, a transformation. The same course content can be perceived completely differently based on price alone.

Pricing isn't just what you charge. It's what you signal. Low prices signal low value, even if the content is excellent.

Price the Outcome, Not the Content

Most creators price based on how much content the course contains. "I have 12 hours of video and 8 modules, so it's worth $97." This is the wrong framing.

Buyers don't pay for hours of content. They pay for the result the content produces.

Ask yourself one question: what is the outcome of this course worth to the buyer?

  • If your course teaches people how to land their first freelance client, that client is worth $2,000-5,000 to them. Suddenly $297 for your course is a steal.
  • If your course teaches people how to grow their Instagram from 0 to 10K, what would they pay an agency for that growth? $500/month? Your $197 course is a no-brainer.
  • If your course teaches people how to negotiate a $20K raise, $497 is a rounding error compared to the value delivered.

When you price based on the value of the transformation rather than the volume of content, suddenly higher prices feel reasonable instead of greedy.

The Course Pricing Framework

Here's a practical pricing framework that works for most online courses in 2026, organized by scope and outcome.

Mini-Course: $27 – $67

A focused course that teaches one specific skill in 1-2 hours. Examples: "How to Edit Reels Like a Pro," "Email Marketing Setup for Coaches," "Beginner's Guide to Lightroom Presets." These are quick wins for buyers who need to learn one thing fast. Low commitment, high completion rates.

Standard Course: $97 – $297

A comprehensive course covering a full topic in 3-8 hours, with multiple modules and clear progression. Examples: "Build Your Personal Brand from Zero," "Complete Guide to Brand Deal Negotiation," "Sell Your First Digital Product." This is where most course creators should anchor their pricing.

Flagship Course: $497 – $1,997

A deep, transformative course that promises a major outcome. Often includes 10+ hours of video, downloadable resources, and sometimes community access. Examples: "Become a Six-Figure Freelance Designer," "Build a YouTube Channel That Pays You." Reserve this tier for after you've validated demand at lower price points.

Premium Programs: $2,000 – $10,000+

Courses paired with live coaching, group calls, or 1:1 access. The high price reflects access, not content alone. Most creators shouldn't start here, but it's worth knowing this tier exists for high-touch transformations.

Common Pricing Mistakes to Avoid

Pricing too low because you feel like an imposter. Imposter syndrome doesn't pay your bills. The market decides if your price is reasonable, not your inner critic. Set the price based on outcome, then let buyers vote with their wallets.

Offering too many price tiers. Three pricing tiers feel professional but they confuse buyers. Confused buyers don't buy. Stick with one main offer, plus maybe a premium upsell with extras like 1:1 calls or templates.

Discounting on day one. Permanent "50% off" pricing is the same as charging the lower price. If your course is worth $297, sell it at $297. Save discounts for genuine launches, special promotions, or strategic moments — not as a default.

Charging monthly for evergreen content. If your course content is fixed (recorded videos, set lessons), don't charge monthly. Buyers feel locked in to content they'll consume in a week. Charge once, give lifetime access. Save subscription pricing for genuinely recurring value (community, ongoing coaching, fresh content).

Free updates forever. Sounds generous but it devalues your future work. If you significantly update the course in two years, that's a separate purchase or upgrade. Lifetime access to the version they bought is reasonable. Lifetime access to all future versions is not.

How to Test if Your Price Is Right

Once you've launched, watch for signals that your price is correct.

Signs your price is too low:

  • Easy sales with no objections
  • Buyers asking why it's so cheap
  • High refund requests for "not what I expected" reasons (low price attracted wrong audience)
  • Profitable on volume but burning out from support

Signs your price is too high:

  • Strong interest, lots of questions, but few purchases
  • Cart abandonment at the payment step
  • Repeated "is there a payment plan?" requests with no follow-through
  • Audience size and engagement don't translate to sales

Signs your price is right:

  • Steady sales without heavy discounting
  • Buyers reference the value they got, not the price they paid
  • Testimonials focus on outcomes
  • Refund rate under 5%

Selling Your Course Without Losing Revenue to Fees

Pricing is only half the equation. Where you sell your course determines how much of that price you actually keep.

Many course platforms take 5-30% of every sale on top of payment processing. That means a course priced at $197 might net you $130 after the platform's cut. For a creator selling 100 courses a month, that's $6,700 left on the table per year.

The smarter approach is to sell directly using a platform that doesn't take a transaction cut. Stripe still charges its standard ~2.9% + 30¢ to process the payment, but no additional platform fee on top.

Every percent you give to a platform is a percent you don't reinvest in your business.

The Bottom Line

Your knowledge is valuable. The years you spent learning, failing, and building expertise — that matters. The transformation you offer to someone two years behind you — that's worth money.

Don't let imposter syndrome drag your price down to "safe." Don't let comparison ("but other courses are cheaper") trap you. Price based on outcome, anchor your tier to your scope, and let the market tell you if you've calibrated correctly.

If the price you're picking makes you slightly uncomfortable, that's probably the right number.
