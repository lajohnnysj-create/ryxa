Best Carrd Alternatives in 2026

Ryxa 📅 April 2026 ⏱ 8 min read

Carrd has been a quiet favorite for years. It's clean, cheap, and gets out of your way. For a one-page personal site or a simple landing page, it's hard to beat.

But Carrd has limits. There's no built-in way to sell a digital product. No way to collect emails without bolting on a third-party tool. No store, no booking, no media kit. The moment your needs grow past "just a page," you start hunting for something else.

Here are five Carrd alternatives worth knowing about in 2026 — what each one does best, who it's built for, and where it falls short.

The right tool depends on what you actually need to do — not just where you'll start.

Alternative #2

Bio Sites by Squarespace

Best for: minimalists who want polish out of the box

Bio Sites is Squarespace's free link-in-bio offering. It's clean, simple, and slightly more design-forward than most link-in-bio tools. Templates feel curated rather than generic, and the editor is intuitive even if you've never built a page before.

Where it shines is the design side — the typography, the spacing, the default themes all feel professional. You can connect a custom domain, embed videos, and link to your social channels with minimal effort.

The catch is depth. Bio Sites is intentionally light. There's no built-in product checkout, no booking, no media kit, no email marketing. If you outgrow it, you're nudged toward a full Squarespace site, which is a much bigger commitment in price and complexity.

Best for: people who want a clean, free landing page that looks better than the default Carrd template.

Where it differs from Carrd: more curated design but less customization control. You trade flexibility for polish.

Alternative #3

Linktree

Best for: anyone who just needs the most popular link-in-bio

Linktree is the household name in link-in-bio. Over 50 million users have built one. It's the safe default — every creator's first stop. The free tier handles the basics (unlimited links, simple analytics, QR codes), and the design is recognizable enough that visitors instantly know what they're looking at.

The product has expanded to include shop integrations, email collection, and digital product sales. Pricing tiers run from free to $35/month for Premium, with transaction fees on commerce that range from 12% on free down to 0% on Premium.

Where Linktree falls short is depth and customization. Free pages still show Linktree branding. Paid tiers add features but not the all-in-one experience creators increasingly want. And the fees on lower tiers eat into earnings if you're selling digital products at any volume.

Best for: anyone who just wants a working link page in five minutes and doesn't need to monetize through it.

Where it differs from Carrd: Linktree is built specifically for the bio link use case. Carrd is more of a general-purpose one-page builder.

Alternative #4

Beacons

Best for: creators who want AI-powered tools baked in

Beacons takes the link-in-bio idea and pushes it toward the all-in-one creator platform space. You get a customizable page, digital product storefront, email marketing, media kit builder, and an AI assistant that helps with brand outreach and content generation.

The platform leans heavily into automation. AI brand outreach, AI-generated emails, auto-DM features for Instagram. For creators who want their tools to do more of the work, Beacons has built a real lane here.

The free tier comes with 9% transaction fees on sales. To drop fees to 0%, you'll need their Store Pro plan at $30/month or Business Pro at $90/month. The Creator Pro at $10/month sits in between with custom domain support and more AI credits, but keeps the 9% fee.

Best for: creators who want AI tools and brand-deal automation without piecing together a separate stack.

Where it differs from Carrd: built for monetization and audience growth from the start; Carrd doesn't try to do either.

Alternative #5

Notion Sites

Best for: people who already live inside Notion

Notion Sites lets you publish any Notion page as a public website. If you already use Notion to organize your work, your portfolio, or your project notes, you can turn that same workspace into a personal site without learning a new tool.

The big draw is content management. Updating your site is the same as editing a doc. You don't think about HTML, layouts, or builders. You write, and the page updates.

Where it falls short is design control. Notion's site styling is intentionally limited. You can't deeply customize fonts, layouts, or transitions the way you can with Carrd or Squarespace. It's a content-first tool, not a design-first one. There's also no commerce, no link-in-bio formatting, no media kit — just clean, readable pages.

Best for: writers, knowledge workers, and people whose website is mostly text and links.

Where it differs from Carrd: Notion's content-first approach beats Carrd for text-heavy sites; Carrd wins on visual flexibility.

How to actually choose

The honest test: ask yourself what you'll use the page for.

If it's a static landing page for a project or a personal site that won't change much, Carrd or Notion Sites are probably enough. Bio Sites if you want a slightly more polished feel.

If you're a creator and the page is your storefront — where people buy your products, book your time, and find your media kit — you'll outgrow a one-page builder fast. That's where Ryxa or Beacons become the better answer. The page becomes one part of a larger system rather than the whole thing.

The trap to avoid: picking a tool because it's the cheapest or simplest, then spending a year duct-taping third-party tools to fill the gaps. Pick the platform that matches where you're actually heading, not just where you are today.

A free one-page site that costs you ten hours of integrations isn't free.
