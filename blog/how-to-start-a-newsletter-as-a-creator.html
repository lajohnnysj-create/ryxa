How to Start a Newsletter as a Creator

Johnny L 10 min read

If you've watched the creator economy long enough, you've heard the same advice on repeat: start a newsletter. Own your audience. Don't rent your relationships from the algorithm.

It's good advice, and it's also vague enough to be useless. Most people who try to start a newsletter quit within three months. Not because they had nothing to say, but because they got stuck on the platform choice, the topic, the design, the schedule, or the silence of an empty list.

This is the version of "how to start a newsletter" that doesn't pretend it's easy. Real platforms, real tradeoffs, real strategy for what to write and how to keep going when no one is reading yet.

Why a Newsletter Is Worth Starting

The pitch you've heard is true: you own your email list in a way you don't own your Instagram followers, your TikTok audience, or your YouTube subscribers. Algorithm changes can wipe out 80% of your reach overnight on social platforms. Email reaches who it reaches: your subscriber gets the email or doesn't, based on inbox rules they control.

That's the structural reason. The practical reason is more boring: email converts better than social. People who give you their email address have made a small commitment. They're more likely to read what you send, click your links, and buy what you sell. A 1,000-person email list often outperforms a 50,000-follower social account when it comes to actual revenue. (Which is why a newsletter sign-up belongs in every creator's link-in-bio.)

That said: a newsletter is a real ongoing commitment. Skip the next sections if you're looking for a quick win. There isn't one here.

Step 1: Pick a Topic You Can Sustain

The hardest thing about a newsletter isn't the first issue. It's the fiftieth.

Most newsletters die because the creator picked a topic they could write about for two weeks but not two years. The topic that survives is one where you have an unusual depth of perspective, ongoing curiosity, and a real audience you want to serve.

Useful prompts to find your topic:

  • What do friends ask you about? The thing people DM you for advice on is a strong signal. You've been informally consulting on this topic for years.
  • What do you read about for fun? If you devour content on a subject, you'll have something to say about it for a long time.
  • What's a niche-of-a-niche? "Marketing" is too broad. "Email marketing for SaaS startups in their first year" is specific enough to own.
  • What perspective is missing? If you've been frustrated reading the existing newsletters in your space, that frustration is your angle.

The topic doesn't have to be genius. It has to be specific, sustainable, and something you actually care about. "Genius" is overrated; "consistent for two years" is what actually builds audiences.

Step 2: Pick a Platform

This is where most people overthink. The platform matters less than your consistency, but the wrong choice can create friction that kills momentum. Here's the honest version of the major options as of 2026:

Substack

The simplest path to publishing. Free to set up, hosted website included, built-in payment processing for paid subscriptions. Strong network effects from cross-recommendations between newsletters. Discovery features built in.

The cost: Substack takes 10% of paid subscription revenue, plus Stripe's standard processing fee (around 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction). On a $5/month subscription, that's a real bite. Free newsletters are completely free to host.

Who it's for: Writers prioritizing simplicity over control. People who want to start writing today without thinking about infrastructure.

Beehiiv

Built specifically for newsletters as a business. Strong analytics, growth tools (referral programs, recommendations, ad network), and on paid plans, no platform fee on subscription revenue.

The cost: Free plan exists with limits. Paid plans are needed to unlock most monetization features.

Who it's for: Creators treating the newsletter as a real business, with monetization plans beyond just paid subscriptions.

Kit (formerly ConvertKit)

Email marketing platform that's pivoted heavily toward creators. Free for up to 10,000 subscribers, the most generous free tier of any major platform. Strong automation features, integrated digital product sales, and a built-in creator network for cross-promotion.

The cost: Free up to 10k subscribers. Paid plans start around $15/month for additional features. 3.5% + $0.30 transaction fee on paid newsletter subscriptions, significantly lower than Substack's 10%.

Who it's for: Creators who want a newsletter plus a business stack: selling products, running automations, managing different audience segments.

Ghost

Open-source platform that combines a CMS, blog, and newsletter in one. Either self-hosted (free, technical) or hosted via Ghost(Pro) starting around $9/month. 0% platform fee on paid subscriptions.

The cost: Higher learning curve. Worth it at scale because you keep all the revenue.

Who it's for: Creators who want a real owned website plus a newsletter, and don't want to give up a percentage at scale.

Buttondown

Minimalist, Markdown-first newsletter platform. Free for the first 100 subscribers. Paid plans from $9/month. 0% revenue cut on paid subscriptions.

Who it's for: Writers who think most newsletter platforms are overbuilt. Developers who want API access. Anyone who values simplicity over features.

Which to actually pick

Honest framework:

  • Just want to start writing this weekend? Substack. The simplicity is worth the future revenue cut. You can always migrate later.
  • Already have an audience and plan to monetize seriously? Beehiiv or Kit. Better tools, better economics at scale.
  • Want full ownership and a real website? Ghost.
  • Want minimum tools, maximum focus on writing? Buttondown.

The most expensive choice is the one you spend three months deciding on instead of writing.

The platform doesn't matter as much as starting. The newsletters that succeed are the ones that exist long enough to compound.

Step 3: Define Your Format

Before you write the first issue, decide a few structural things. They'll save you weeks of decision fatigue later.

  • Length. Short (300-500 words) is easier to write and easier to read. Long (1500+ words) requires more skill and more time but builds deeper trust. Pick a range you can actually sustain.
  • Frequency. Weekly is the gold standard for most newsletters. Daily is intense but works for news-style content. Monthly is fine for in-depth pieces but builds habit slowly. Pick the frequency you can hit even on a bad week.
  • Day and time. Pick one and stick to it. Tuesday at 9am, Sunday at 7pm, doesn't matter, but consistency builds expectation. Subscribers who know when your email arrives are more likely to actually read it.
  • Voice. Conversational works for most creators. Formal/corporate works for certain B2B niches. Whatever you pick, write the way you actually talk. Trying to sound smart is the most common reason newsletters feel boring.

Step 4: Write Your First Five Issues Before Launch

This is the single most under-discussed advice for new newsletters. Don't launch with one issue and a promise. Write five issues (or at minimum three) before you publish anything.

Here's why: the gap between issue 1 and issue 2 is where most newsletters die. You've used your best idea on issue 1. Now it's a week later, life is busy, and you have nothing ready. You skip a week. The week after, you skip again. Subscribers forget you. Two months later, you quietly delete the newsletter.

Having a backlog buys you cushion. When week 3 arrives and you're swamped, issue 3 is already written. You buy yourself another week to write issue 4. The buffer means consistency isn't a heroic effort.

Step 5: Set Up the Basics

Before your first issue ships, these things should be in place:

  1. A signup page. All major platforms generate one for you. Make sure the value proposition is clear in one sentence: "What will I get if I subscribe?"
  2. A welcome email. Triggered automatically when someone subscribes. Set expectations: when emails come, what they'll cover, what to do if they don't see them.
  3. A way to share. Add the signup link to your Instagram bio, Twitter bio, LinkedIn, email signature, and any other place people might find you.
  4. A name and basic branding. Doesn't have to be elaborate. A clean wordmark and one accent color is plenty for issue 1. Don't get stuck designing.
  5. Your first email's "from" name. Use your real name, not a brand name. Open rates are dramatically higher when emails come from a person.

Step 6: Grow the List

The hardest part. Newsletter growth is slower than social growth. There's no algorithm pushing your content to strangers. Every subscriber requires a deliberate decision.

Tactics that actually work:

  • Promote on social, repeatedly. Mention your newsletter in your Instagram bio, in story highlights, at the end of every Reel, in Threads posts. Most followers won't see any single mention; you need to repeat.
  • Lead magnet. A free downloadable resource (PDF guide, template, checklist) tied to your topic, in exchange for an email. Conversion rates from social to email are dramatically higher with a lead magnet than a generic "subscribe to my newsletter."
  • Cross-promotion with other newsletters. If you can find newsletters with adjacent audiences, swap shoutouts. Most platforms have referral or recommendation features built in.
  • Referral programs. Beehiiv, Kit, and others have built-in referral systems where existing subscribers invite friends. Adds compounding growth once you have an early base.
  • Embed signup forms in your website. Every page should have a way to subscribe. Footer, sidebar, exit intent popup if you can stand them.
  • Capture emails from everything you already do. Every link-in-bio click, every digital product sale, every course enrollment, every booking. All of those are moments where someone is already engaging with you and willing to share their email. Tools that capture marketing consent at those touchpoints (Ryxa does this natively across link-in-bio, courses, coaching, and digital products) can build your list passively in the background while you focus on creating.
  • Mention specific past issues, not the newsletter generally. "I wrote about X this week, link in bio" outperforms "subscribe to my newsletter" by a wide margin. People subscribe to specific value, not generic future value.

Step 7: Plan to Make Nothing for a While

Most successful creator newsletters made zero dollars for the first 6-12 months. The ones that monetize early often do so by accident (a sponsor reaches out, or a paid product happens to fit). Don't expect monetization until you have a real list (usually 1,000+ engaged subscribers minimum) and a real reason for them to pay.

Common revenue paths once you've built a list:

  • Paid subscriptions. Upgrade some content to paid-only. Substack, Beehiiv, Kit, and Ghost all support this natively.
  • Sponsorships. Brands pay to be mentioned in your newsletter. Rates are loosely tied to subscriber count and engagement, but vary wildly by niche.
  • Selling your own products. Courses, ebooks, templates, services. Email lists tend to convert better than social audiences for product sales. (A guide on how to price a course.)
  • Affiliate income. Recommending tools and getting commissions. Works best when the recommendation is genuinely useful.

Common Mistakes

  • Picking a topic you don't actually care about just because it has search volume. You will quit within six issues.
  • Designing the perfect template before issue 1. The template won't matter; the writing will. Ship something basic and improve over time.
  • Trying to grow a list before you have a newsletter. Subscribers signing up to a newsletter that doesn't exist yet often forget by the time you launch.
  • Skipping weeks early. Skipping issue 4 trains your readers that the schedule is unreliable. A reliable cadence (even at low quality early) beats sporadic brilliance.
  • Apologizing for being late. If you skip a week, just don't mention it. "Sorry I missed last week" reminds everyone you're inconsistent.
  • Treating it as a side project forever. Newsletters that get serious attention from their creator outperform newsletters that get squeezed in between everything else.

The Realistic Timeline

If you want a sense of what this actually looks like:

  • Months 1-3: 0-200 subscribers. Mostly friends and existing followers. The audience feels invisible. Most quitting happens here.
  • Months 4-6: 200-1,000 subscribers if you're consistent and promoting actively. First sponsor inquiries possible if niche is right.
  • Months 7-12: 1,000-5,000 subscribers if growth is steady. Real monetization conversations start. Newsletter starts to feel like a real thing.
  • Year 2+: Compounding kicks in. Network effects from cross-promotion. This is where serious creators build something real.

None of these are guaranteed. Some newsletters explode, some plateau, some never break 100 subscribers. The variable that matters most is whether you keep showing up.

The Honest Bottom Line

A newsletter is one of the highest-leverage things a creator can build. It's also one of the slowest. There's no algorithmic shortcut, no viral moment that fixes a stagnant list, no tool that replaces the work of writing something useful every week for years.

The creators who build great newsletters aren't more talented than the ones who quit. They just kept going past the part where it felt pointless. If you can do that, if you can write issue 47 with the same care as issue 4 even when the subscriber count is barely growing, you'll end up with something almost no other creator has: a direct line to people who actually want to hear from you.

Pick a topic. Pick a platform. Write five issues. Hit publish. The hardest part is starting. The second hardest part is not stopping.
