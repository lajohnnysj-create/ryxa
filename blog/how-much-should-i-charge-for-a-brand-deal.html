How Much Should I Charge for a Brand Deal?

The brand sent the DM. You opened it. They want a sponsored post. They asked for your rates. And now you're staring at the keyboard wondering if your answer is going to be too high (and lose the deal) or too low (and leave money on the table forever).

This is one of the most-Googled questions in the creator economy, and almost every answer you find online is bad. The advice is either oversimplified ("just charge $100 per 10,000 followers"), wildly inflated ("here's a 7-figure influencer's rate card"), or so hedged it tells you nothing. Brand deals are one of the most reliable income streams for creators (something we covered in how to make money on Instagram), but only if you can actually price them.

Here's the honest version. Real benchmarks, the factors that actually move the number, and what to do when the brand offers less than what you quoted.

The $100-per-10K Rule Is Wrong

You've seen this rule everywhere. "Charge $100 per 10,000 followers." It's the default answer when nobody knows the right answer, and it's misleading for almost every creator who uses it.

The rule undercharges niche creators with engaged audiences, overcharges creators in saturated low-CPM categories, ignores deliverables completely, and treats every follower as worth the same regardless of platform or geography. A 50,000-follower fitness creator on Instagram is not worth $500 per post. A 50,000-follower B2B SaaS creator on LinkedIn might be worth $5,000.

The rule survives because creators want a formula and brands want a baseline. Both groups treat it as gospel even though neither group's actual deals match it. The real number depends on what you're delivering, to whom, and how much that audience is worth to a specific brand.

The Real Benchmarks (2026)

Here are the rate ranges that creators actually report seeing in 2026. These are broad because the variables are real, but they're directional. If your number is wildly outside these ranges in either direction, something's off.

Instagram (per post or carousel)

  • 1K–10K followers: $50 to $250
  • 10K–50K followers: $250 to $1,200
  • 50K–250K followers: $1,200 to $5,000
  • 250K–1M followers: $5,000 to $20,000
  • 1M+ followers: $20,000 and up, often much higher

Stories tend to run 30 to 50 percent of an in-feed post rate. Reels often run 1.5 to 2x a static post rate because of higher reach. A full campaign (feed post plus Reel plus 3 Stories) is usually quoted as a bundle at 1.8 to 2.5x the in-feed rate, not the sum of individual rates.

TikTok (per video)

  • 10K–50K followers: $200 to $1,000
  • 50K–250K followers: $1,000 to $4,000
  • 250K–1M followers: $4,000 to $15,000
  • 1M+ followers: $15,000 and up

TikTok rates often run slightly below Instagram at the same follower count because organic reach is less correlated with follower count. A 50K TikTok creator whose last five videos averaged 500K views can charge significantly more than the bracket suggests. Brands pay for views and engagement, not follower count alone.

YouTube (per dedicated or integrated video)

  • Integrated mention (60 to 90 seconds): roughly $20 to $40 per 1,000 average video views
  • Dedicated video: roughly $50 to $100 per 1,000 average video views

YouTube is the one platform where the math actually works on views, not followers. A YouTuber with 100K subscribers whose videos consistently get 500K views is worth more than one with 500K subscribers whose videos get 50K. Average views per video over the last 30 to 90 days is the metric that matters, not subscriber count.

YouTube Shorts and Instagram Reels

Short-form vertical video rates have compressed across platforms. Expect roughly 60 to 80 percent of a TikTok rate at equivalent reach. If you're posting the same content across all three platforms, brands often pay for a "vertical video bundle" at about 1.4 to 1.8x a single-platform rate, not 3x.

LinkedIn (per post)

LinkedIn rates are wildly higher than equivalent follower counts on other platforms because the audience is decision-makers and the CPMs are higher. A 20K-follower LinkedIn creator in B2B can often charge $1,500 to $4,000 per sponsored post, sometimes more for thought-leadership-style content.

Newsletter (per send)

Roughly $30 to $80 per 1,000 active subscribers for a dedicated section, $80 to $150 per 1,000 for a primary sponsorship. Newsletter rates have climbed sharply in 2025 and 2026 as brands have learned that owned email audiences outperform social on conversion.

What Actually Moves the Number

Two creators with the same follower count can have legitimately different rates. Here are the factors that justify the variance.

Engagement rate

A 50K creator with 8 percent engagement is worth significantly more than a 50K creator with 1.5 percent engagement. Brands have learned to look. Engagement rate tells them whether your audience is alive or whether your follower count is a vanity number. Get a clean engagement number (likes plus comments divided by followers, averaged across your last 10 posts) and have it ready.

If your engagement is high but your follower count is modest, you can often charge above the bracket. If it's the other way around, expect brands to push back on your rate, and they'll be right to.

Niche

Some niches command higher CPMs because the buyer's audience is more valuable. B2B, finance, tech, business, and high-ticket lifestyle (luxury, real estate, weddings) tend to pay above-bracket. Beauty, fashion, fitness, and food tend to pay at-bracket because they're saturated. Hobby niches with low commercial intent (most meme accounts, broad lifestyle content) tend to pay below-bracket.

This isn't fairness. It's economics. The brand isn't paying for your time. They're paying for access to your audience, and some audiences are simply more lucrative to advertise to.

Geography

An audience that's 80 percent US-based is more valuable to most brands than an audience that's spread across 60 countries. A US-heavy audience can support rates 30 to 100 percent higher than a globally diluted one at the same follower count. If your audience is primarily in a market that's not on the brand's target list, expect a lower rate.

Deliverables

Most creators undercharge for deliverables. A single static post is one thing. A static post, plus three Stories, plus a Reel, plus a usage rights clause that lets the brand boost the content as paid media for 6 months, plus a 30-day exclusivity clause where you can't post a competitor's product is an entirely different deal.

Itemize your deliverables. Charge separately for each one. Bundle pricing only as a discount, never as a default.

Usage rights and whitelisting

This is the single most common place where creators leave money on the table. If a brand wants to use your content as paid media (i.e., they run ads with your face and content), that's a separate fee on top of the post fee. Typical add-ons:

  • Organic usage on the brand's owned channels for 30 days: usually free or a small add-on
  • Organic usage in perpetuity: 15 to 25 percent on top of base rate
  • Paid media usage for 30 days (whitelisting): 25 to 50 percent on top
  • Paid media usage for 6 months: 50 to 100 percent on top
  • Paid media usage in perpetuity: 100 to 200 percent on top, and try to avoid it

Brands often try to fold "all usage rights" into the original quote. Don't let them. Usage rights are the single highest-leverage line item in a brand deal.

Exclusivity

If the brand wants you to not post a competitor's product for 30, 60, or 90 days, that's revenue you're forfeiting. Charge for it. A 30-day category exclusivity clause should add 15 to 30 percent to the base fee. A 90-day clause should add 30 to 60 percent. A 6-month or 12-month exclusivity (rare but real) should add at least 50 to 100 percent.

The first quote a brand gives you is rarely their ceiling. The first quote you give a brand should never be your floor.

How to Set Your Number

Here's the actual process, end to end.

Start with a base rate, not a per-post rate

Pick a single platform you're most active on. Find your bracket above. Anchor to the midpoint as your baseline (e.g., 30K Instagram = baseline of $700 to $800). This is your "single in-feed post, organic only, no usage rights, no exclusivity" rate.

Adjust for your specific situation

Apply modifiers to your base:

  • High engagement rate (above 5 percent): add 30 to 50 percent
  • Low engagement rate (below 1.5 percent): subtract 30 to 50 percent
  • High-CPM niche (B2B, finance, luxury): add 50 to 100 percent
  • Low-commercial-intent niche: subtract 20 to 40 percent
  • US-dominant audience for US brand: add 20 to 30 percent
  • Audience demographically mismatched to brand: subtract 20 to 40 percent

This gives you a defensible number. When the brand pushes back, you can show your work. "My rate is $X because my engagement is 6.2 percent and my audience is 78 percent US-based and 25 to 34 years old."

Add for deliverables

Now stack on top of base:

  • Stories: 30 to 50 percent of base per Story
  • Reel/Short: 1.5 to 2x of base
  • Carousel: 1.2 to 1.4x of base
  • Cross-platform repost (TikTok of same content): 40 to 60 percent of base

Add for usage and exclusivity

Layer in usage rights and exclusivity per the percentages above. This is the layer most creators forget. It's also the layer where a $1,000 deal can legitimately turn into a $2,500 deal.

Always quote a range, never a single number

When a brand asks for your rates, never reply with a single dollar amount. Quote a range and tie it to deliverables. "For a single in-feed post with 30 days of organic usage, my rate is $800 to $1,200 depending on scope. Reel adds, Stories adds, and exclusivity adds increase that range. I can put together a specific quote once I know the full ask."

This signals you've done this before, gives you negotiation room, and forces the brand to specify what they actually want before you commit to a number.

When the Brand Comes In Low

Most brands will counter your quote. This is normal. It's not personal. It's procurement doing its job. Here's how to handle it.

Don't drop your rate. Drop scope.

If they offer $400 against your $800 quote, don't accept $400 for the same deliverables. Counter with $400 for less. "I can do $400 for a single Story instead of the full feed post. Or $700 for the feed post if we drop the usage rights." This trains the brand that your rate is fixed but scope is flexible.

If they say "we have a budget of $X"

This is almost always a negotiation tactic, not a hard ceiling. If $X is meaningfully below your number, ask: "What deliverables does that budget cover? I can scope a campaign to fit it." If their answer is still the full original ask at $X, they're testing you. Decline politely or hold firm on a counter.

If they offer "exposure" instead of pay

Decline. A brand that can't afford to pay creators can't afford to pay anyone. The "exposure" they're offering is worth essentially nothing, and accepting these deals trains brands in your niche that creators in your tier work for free.

The exception: if it's a brand you genuinely love, the product is genuinely useful, and the audience overlap is genuinely strong, you can choose to do it. That's a decision you're making as a fan, not a business decision. Be clear with yourself about which is which.

If they ghost after your quote

Don't chase. Don't lower. A brand that ghosts after seeing your rate either didn't have the budget or wasn't serious. Follow up once a week later with a single line ("Wanted to check if you had any questions on the proposal"). If still nothing, move on. Chasing trains brands that your rate is soft.

One mental shift that helps: brands aren't doing you a favor by offering a deal. They're buying access to your audience. The audience is the asset. You're the gatekeeper. The transaction is symmetric, not a gift from them to you.

What Most Creators Get Wrong

A few specific mistakes that come up over and over in brand deal negotiations.

Quoting too fast

The brand sends an inquiry. You reply within 10 minutes with a number. They feel like you'll take anything. Take a day. Reply professionally. The pause itself is a signal.

Not getting the full ask in writing first

Always get the brief in writing before you quote. Deliverables, platforms, timing, usage rights, exclusivity, content approval process, and posting window. Many creators quote off a vague verbal ask, then discover halfway through that the brand expected three Stories, paid usage rights, and a 60-day exclusivity that wasn't in the original quote.

Forgetting payment terms

Net-30 from invoice is industry standard. Net-60 and Net-90 are common with larger brands and agencies. Get the payment term in writing. A $5,000 deal that pays Net-90 is meaningfully different from one that pays Net-30. If you can, push for 50 percent upfront or at minimum, payment on posting rather than payment net-X from invoice.

Not tracking deals

Most creators are running their brand deal business out of email threads, screenshots, and memory. By the time you're juggling more than three or four deals a year, this falls apart. Things slip. Invoices get forgotten. Usage rights get violated and nobody notices. A proper CRM for brand deals (or even a basic spreadsheet you keep up to date) prevents the situation where a brand uses your content as paid media for 8 months because nobody on either side remembered the original 30-day clause.

The Rate Card Question

Many creators keep a "rate card" they send to brands. Some swear by it. Others (more common at higher tiers) avoid it entirely. The case for either:

The case for a rate card: it saves negotiation time, anchors the conversation at your number, and signals professionalism. Useful when you're getting a lot of inquiries and want to filter out brands that can't meet your floor.

The case against: it locks you in. Different brands have different value to you, and a fixed rate card prevents you from charging premium rates to brands that should pay premium and prevents you from doing flexible deals with brands you want to work with. Many top-tier creators quote per deal instead.

A reasonable middle ground: have a one-page document that lists your typical deliverables and rate ranges (not fixed prices), shares your audience demographics and engagement rate, and links to a few past brand examples. This is closer to a "media kit" than a rate card and tends to convert better.

What Your Rate Is Actually Telling Brands

One thing most creators miss: your rate is itself a signal. A creator who charges $150 for a Reel and a creator who charges $1,500 for the same Reel are sending entirely different signals to the brand about who they are and what their audience is worth.

Charging too low doesn't just leave money on the table for that deal. It positions you as a low-tier creator, gets you recommended for low-tier campaigns, and quietly caps the brand of brand that will reach out to you in the future. A creator who consistently charges $200 will keep getting $200 inquiries forever. A creator who confidently quotes $1,500 will start getting inquiries from brands who pay $1,500.

Your rate isn't just compensation. It's positioning.

The Realistic Bottom Line

There is no single right answer to "how much should I charge for a brand deal." There is a defensible answer for your specific situation, based on your platform, follower count, engagement rate, niche, audience geography, and the specific deliverables and usage rights being asked for.

Start with the bracket above. Anchor to the midpoint. Apply the modifiers honestly. Add for deliverables and usage. Quote a range. Negotiate by dropping scope, not rate. Track the deal somewhere durable so it doesn't slip.

And remember that the first deal you do at a given rate sets the floor for every deal after it. It's worth a little more friction now to set the floor where you actually want it.
