What to Do If Your Instagram Gets Hacked

Johnny L 11 min read

For most people, a hacked Instagram account is an inconvenience. For a creator, it can be a direct hit to their income. The account is the storefront, the audience, the portfolio, and the proof of work all at once. So when the login stops working, the panic is real and it is rational.

This guide is the calm version. It walks through what is actually happening when an account gets compromised, the recovery steps in the order that works, what to do in the worst case where the attacker changed your email and phone number, and how to lock everything down afterward so it does not happen again. None of this requires technical skill. It requires doing the steps in the right order and not skipping the slow ones.

First, Confirm It Is Actually Hacked

Before starting recovery, rule out the boring explanations, because they are common and they have faster fixes.

  • You are simply logged out. Instagram occasionally logs people out after app updates or security checks. Try logging back in with your known password first.
  • The app is glitching. Force-close it, reopen it, or try logging in from a browser at instagram.com. A surprising number of "I am hacked" moments are an app that needs restarting.
  • You forgot a recent password change. If you changed it on another device and forgot, the password reset flow solves it in two minutes.

It is genuinely hacked if you see signs like: you cannot log in with the correct password, you got an email from Instagram saying your email address or password was changed and it was not you, friends report DMs or posts you did not send, or your profile photo, bio, or username suddenly changed. If any of those are true, move to recovery now. Speed matters, because the longer an attacker holds the account, the more they can change.

If you received an email from Instagram saying "your email was changed" and it includes a link that says "revert this change" or "secure my account," click that link immediately. It is the single fastest recovery path and it often expires within a short window. Do this before anything else in this guide.

The Recovery Steps, In Order

Instagram's recovery system has several paths. They work best when you try them in this sequence rather than jumping around.

Step 1: Try the standard password reset

On the login screen, tap "Forgot password." Enter your username, the email you originally used, or your phone number. If the attacker has not yet changed your contact details, you will get a reset link or code. Use it, set a new strong password, and you are most of the way back. This is the path that resolves the majority of compromises caught early.

Step 2: Use the in-app "Get help logging in" flow

If the password reset does not reach you, go back to the login screen and look for "Get more help" or "Need more help." This routes you into Instagram's identity-verification flow. For accounts without photos of a person, it may ask for the email or phone the account was created with. For accounts with photos of you, it may ask you to take a video selfie so Meta can match it against the photos on the account. Follow whichever path it offers. The video selfie path is slow but it is reliable, because it proves you are the real person behind the account.

Step 3: Request a support code from a trusted device

If you are still logged in on any other device, a second phone, a tablet, an old login in a desktop browser, do not log out of it. That logged-in session is leverage. From it, go to Settings, then Account Center, then Password and Security, and you can often change the password from there and kick out the attacker's session. Many creators recover purely because they happened to still be logged in somewhere they forgot about.

Step 4: Submit a report through Meta's dedicated hacked-account page

If the in-app flows fail, Meta has a dedicated web page for compromised accounts. Search for "Instagram hacked account" and use the official help center result. It will ask for your account details and walk you through identity verification. This path is slower, sometimes days, but it is built for exactly the case where you have lost access to the email and phone too.

The Worst Case: They Changed Your Email and Phone

This is the scenario creators dread. The attacker logs in, immediately changes the email address and phone number on the account, and turns on their own two-factor authentication. Now every recovery code goes to them, not you. It feels hopeless. It is not.

Here is what still works:

  1. The "revert change" email. When an attacker changes your email, Instagram sends a notification to your original email with a link to reverse it. Check that inbox, including spam. This is why Step 1 of recovery is always "check your email." That link is your fastest way back even after the change.
  2. The video selfie verification. If your account has photos of your face, Meta's identity verification can match a video selfie against them. The attacker cannot pass this, because they are not you. This is the single strongest tool a personal-brand creator has, and it is a real argument for having clear photos of yourself on your creator account.
  3. Account creation details. Meta's support flow may ask for the exact email or phone the account was originally created with, even if it is not the current one. If you still have access to that original email, it can prove ownership. Dig up that information now if you can.
  4. Patience with the support process. When the automated flows are exhausted, the manual review path is genuinely slow, and it is frustrating, but creators do get accounts back this way. Submit the report, keep the case reference, and do not submit ten duplicate reports, which can slow things down.

The video selfie is the one verification an attacker cannot fake. If your creator account shows your face clearly, you are far harder to permanently lock out.

While You Wait: Damage Control

Recovery can take time. In the meantime, limit what the attacker can do to your audience and your reputation.

  • Warn your audience from another channel. Post on your other platforms, your story on a backup account, your email list, anywhere you have reach, telling people the account is compromised and not to click links or send money. Attackers frequently DM a creator's followers with crypto scams or fake giveaways. A quick warning protects the people who trust you.
  • Tell anyone with business ties. If you have brand deals in progress, a manager, or collaborators, let them know so they are not blindsided by strange messages from your account.
  • Change passwords on connected accounts. If your Instagram password was reused anywhere else, your email, other social platforms, your bank, change those immediately. Reused passwords are how one breach becomes five.
  • Check your email account first. Often the Instagram hack started with the email being compromised. Secure the email before anything else, or the attacker just resets Instagram again the moment you recover it.

After You Get It Back: Lock It Down

Recovering the account is not the end. The attacker knew a way in, and if you do not close it, they may come back. Do all of these the same day you regain access.

  1. Change the password to something new and unique. Not a variation of the old one. A long, random password that exists nowhere else. A password manager makes this painless.
  2. Turn on two-factor authentication, using an authenticator app. Two-factor is the single biggest barrier to a repeat hack. Use an authenticator app rather than SMS where possible, because SMS codes can be intercepted through SIM-swap attacks. This is the step that actually prevents the next incident.
  3. Review and remove unknown logged-in sessions. In Settings, under Account Center and Password and Security, check "Where you are logged in." Log out every device and location you do not recognize.
  4. Check authorized third-party apps. Attackers sometimes connect a malicious app so they keep access even after a password change. Revoke anything you do not recognize or no longer use.
  5. Re-verify your email and phone. Confirm the account's email and phone number are yours and are themselves secured with strong passwords and two-factor.
  6. Save your recovery codes. When you enable two-factor, Instagram gives you backup codes. Save them somewhere safe and offline. They are how you get back in if you ever lose your authenticator.

How Creators Get Hacked in the First Place

Understanding the common entry points helps you avoid the next one. Almost every creator hack traces back to one of these:

  • Phishing DMs and emails. A message that looks like it is from Instagram, or from a brand, saying your account has a copyright strike or needs verification, with a link to a fake login page. You enter your password and the attacker has it. Real platform communications do not work this way. We covered the business-facing version of this in how to spot fake Instagram followers, and the same skepticism applies to any unexpected message.
  • Reused passwords. Your password leaked in some unrelated website's data breach years ago, and you used the same one on Instagram. Attackers buy these lists and try them everywhere.
  • Fake brand-deal offers. An attacker poses as a brand, sends a "contract" or "media kit portal" link, and harvests your login when you sign in to view it. If a partnership opportunity asks you to log in through a link they provide, stop.
  • SIM swapping. An attacker convinces your mobile carrier to move your number to their SIM, then receives your SMS two-factor codes. This is why an authenticator app beats SMS.
  • Granting access to a sketchy "growth" tool. Apps that promise more followers often ask for your login or a broad connection, then either misuse it or get breached themselves.

The pattern across all of these: the attacker either tricks you into handing over credentials, or reuses credentials you exposed elsewhere. Strong unique passwords plus authenticator-app two-factor closes the large majority of these doors.

The Bigger Lesson: Do Not Build Only on Rented Land

A hacked account is a brutal reminder of a structural truth in the creator economy. Your social platforms are rented land. You do not own the audience, the platform does, and access can be taken from you, by an attacker or by the platform itself, with little warning and slow recourse.

That is not a reason to abandon Instagram. It is a reason to make sure it is not the only thing holding your business together. Creators who recover from a hack with minimal damage tend to have the same things in common: an email list they own outright, a presence on more than one platform, and a home base that is theirs. This is the same argument we made in do I need a website if I have Instagram, and a hacked account is the moment it stops being theoretical. A link in bio page that you control, with your own links and your own storefront, keeps functioning and keeps earning even on a day when your Instagram login does not. The goal is simple: no single account, controlled by someone else, should be able to take down your whole livelihood.

The Bottom Line

If your Instagram is hacked right now: check your original email for a "revert this change" link, try the password reset, then the in-app "get more help" flow, then the video selfie verification, then Meta's dedicated hacked-account report, in that order. Warn your audience from another channel while you wait. When you get the account back, change the password, turn on authenticator-app two-factor, and kick out every session and app you do not recognize, all on the same day.

And once the immediate fire is out, take the quieter lesson seriously. Secure the account properly, and make sure it is not the only pillar your creator business stands on. The creators who weather these incidents best are the ones who were never fully dependent on a single login in the first place.
