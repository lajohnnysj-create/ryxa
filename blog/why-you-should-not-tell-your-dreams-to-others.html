Why You Should Never Tell People Your Dreams

Johnny L 6 min read

You just had an idea. Maybe it's a course you want to build. A business you want to launch. A brand you want to create from nothing. You're excited, so you do what most people do. You tell someone about it.

And that's where it starts to fall apart.

Not because the idea was bad. Not because you weren't ready. But because the moment you shared it with the wrong person, something shifted. Their reaction planted a seed of doubt that wasn't there before.

The Psychology Behind It

There's a well-known study from NYU professor Peter Gollwitzer that found something counterintuitive: people who announce their goals to others are less likely to follow through. When you tell someone your plans and they acknowledge them, even with a simple nod, your brain registers a sense of completion. You feel like you've already done something, even though you haven't done anything yet.

That feeling of progress is an illusion. You talked about building a course. You talked about launching a product. You talked about changing your life. But talking is not doing, and your brain can't always tell the difference.

Your brain doesn't know the difference between talking about your dream and actually working on it. Both feel like progress. Only one is real.

Not Everyone Wants to See You Win

This is the part nobody wants to hear. The people closest to you (friends, family, coworkers) are not always rooting for you. Not because they're bad people. But because your ambition can make them uncomfortable.

When you tell someone you're building something, you're inadvertently holding a mirror up to their own life. If they're stuck in a job they hate, if they gave up on their own dreams, if they've settled, your fire reminds them of what they didn't do. And instead of being inspired, some people feel threatened.

That's when the "advice" comes in.

"That's a lot of work." "The market is too saturated." "You should probably keep your day job." "I just don't want you to get hurt."

It sounds like concern. It feels like love. But it's projection. They're not protecting you from failure. They're protecting themselves from watching you succeed where they didn't try.

The Quiet Builder Always Wins

Look at any creator or entrepreneur who made it. The common thread isn't that they had the most support or the loudest announcement. It's that they built in silence and let the results do the talking. (When you're ready, a clean link in bio is usually the first piece of public-facing infrastructure to set up.)

When you work quietly, three things happen. You remove the pressure of other people's expectations. You eliminate the noise of unsolicited opinions. And you give yourself the freedom to fail, pivot, and try again without anyone watching.

Nobody needs to know you rewrote your course outline four times. Nobody needs to know your first launch flopped. Nobody needs to know you stayed up until 3 AM fixing a bug on your website. Those are your chapters to write privately.

Build in silence. Let your results make the noise.

The Difference Between Support and Sabotage

This doesn't mean you should isolate yourself entirely. There are people who genuinely want you to win, but they're rarer than you think. The key is learning to tell the difference.

Real support sounds like: "How can I help?" or "I believe in you" or "That's going to be hard, but you can do it." Sabotage disguised as love sounds like: "Be realistic" or "Most businesses fail" or "Why would people pay for that?"

A good test: after you share something with someone, do you feel more motivated or less? If you consistently feel deflated after talking to certain people, that's your answer. Protect your energy.

Your Circle Will Change, And That's OK

One of the hardest parts of building something is realizing that your current circle might not be your forever circle. The friends who are great for going out on weekends might not be the ones who understand why you're spending every free hour working on a project that doesn't pay yet. (A related read on the isolation that comes with this work.)

That doesn't make them bad friends. It makes them the wrong audience for this chapter of your life. You don't have to cut people off. You just have to stop sharing certain things with people who can't hold them properly.

Share your wins with people who have earned the right to hear about them. Share your struggles with people who won't use them against you. And keep your biggest, boldest dreams between you and the work itself.

The Work Speaks Louder

There will come a day when people see what you've built and say "I had no idea you were working on this." That moment, the reveal after the quiet grind, is worth more than any premature announcement.

You don't need permission. You don't need validation. You don't need someone to believe in your dream before you start building it. The belief comes from doing the work, not from telling people about the work.

So the next time you get an idea that excites you (the course you want to create, the brand you want to launch, the life you want to build) do yourself a favor.

Don't tell anyone. Just start.
