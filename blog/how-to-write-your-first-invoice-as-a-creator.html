How to Write Your First Invoice as a Creator

You finished your first brand deal. You posted the Reel. The client said "looks great, please send your invoice." And now you are staring at a blank document with no idea what an invoice is supposed to look like.

This is one of those moments where everyone assumes you already know how to do the thing. There is no class on it. Your favorite creators do not post tutorials about it. The internet's advice is mostly written for plumbers and consultants, not creators, and uses terms that do not quite map to how creator deals work.

The good news: an invoice is much simpler than it looks. It is a one-page document that says "here is who I am, here is who you are, here is what I did, here is what you owe me, here is how to pay me." Get those five things right and you have a real invoice. The rest is formatting.

This post walks through exactly what to include, what to leave off, what creator-specific situations to handle (Net 30 timelines, multiple deliverables on one deal, agency middlemen), and what to do when a brand goes silent after you send it. Plus a structural template at the end you can adapt to any tool you want to use.

What an invoice actually has to include

Every invoice, regardless of who is sending it, contains the same core elements. Miss any of them and the brand's accounts payable team will bounce it back, which delays your payment by a week or two while you fix it. Get them all right and the invoice gets paid on schedule.

1. The word "Invoice"

At the top, clearly. Sounds dumb to include this as a rule, but the accounts payable team at a brand processes hundreds of documents a week. If yours does not clearly say INVOICE at the top, it might get misfiled as "creator notes" or "follow-up email" and sit in someone's inbox for a month. Make it obvious.

2. Your information

Who is sending the invoice. This is where creators get tripped up because they assume they need to look like a "real business" with an address and an EIN. You do not.

The minimum: your legal name (the name on your tax forms, not your handle), your email, and your phone number. If you have an LLC or DBA, use the business name and an EIN. If you do not, your legal name and Social Security Number is fine for tax purposes (though you do not put your SSN on the invoice itself, you provide it via a W-9 form separately).

What you do not need: an office address (you can use your home address or just your city and state), a logo (nice but optional), or a "company website" (your social handle is fine).

3. The client's information

Who you are billing. This is also where invoices often go wrong, because the entity on the contract is often not the entity that pays.

If you worked with a brand, you might assume you bill "[Brand Name]." Sometimes that is correct. Often, the brand has hired an agency, and the agency is paying you, not the brand. Or the brand has a parent company, and the parent company's legal name is what goes on the invoice. The contract should tell you which entity to bill. If it does not, ask the brand contact: "Who should the invoice be made out to? Same as the entity on the contract?"

The contact details to include: company legal name, billing address, and the email of the person you submit invoices to (usually accounts payable, sometimes the brand manager themselves on smaller deals).

4. Invoice number

A unique identifier for this invoice. Use any system you want, as long as it is consistent and goes up. Many creators start with INV-001 and number sequentially. If you want a system that includes the year, INV-2026-001 works.

Why this matters: the brand's accounts payable system tracks invoices by number. If you ever have to ask "did you receive invoice INV-005?" you need a number to reference. Without it, you are saying "did you get the thing I sent" and they have to dig through their inbox.

5. Invoice date and due date

Two separate dates. Invoice date is the day you send it. Due date is when you expect to be paid by.

For creator deals, the due date is calculated from the brand's payment terms. "Net 30" means 30 days after the invoice date. Net 45, Net 60, and Net 90 are all common with larger brands and agencies. You do not negotiate this on the invoice (you negotiate it in the contract); you just calculate the due date based on what the contract says.

If you are not sure what the terms are, ask. "Hi, just confirming, are your payment terms Net 30 from invoice date?" is a perfectly normal question, and the answer determines what you put on the invoice.

6. Line items (what you did)

The actual work being billed. Each deliverable gets its own line, with a price.

Bad: "Social media content - $2000"

Good: "1 Instagram Reel, posted [date] - $1500" and "3 Instagram Stories, posted [date] - $500"

Itemizing matters because if there is any later dispute ("we thought we paid for two Reels"), the invoice is the receipt. Vague line items create vague disputes. Specific ones do not.

If your deal included usage rights as a separate fee, that goes on a separate line too: "6-month paid usage rights for above content - $750." Same with kill fees, if you negotiated one and the deal got cancelled: "Cancellation fee per contract clause 7 - $1000."

7. Subtotal, taxes (if applicable), and total

Sum the line items into a subtotal. Apply tax if you are required to charge it (most US creators do not charge sales tax on services to out-of-state clients, but check your state's rules). The total is what the client owes.

For most US creators on most brand deals, there is no tax line. The total is just the subtotal. If you are unsure whether you should be charging sales tax, ask an accountant. The rules vary by state and by service category, and it is one of the more confusing parts of self-employed work.

8. Payment instructions

How the client should pay you. Options:

Bank transfer (ACH). The most common for US creator deals. You provide your bank's routing number and your account number. Some brands will ask you to fill out a separate W-9 + direct deposit form before issuing payment.

PayPal, Venmo, or other digital wallets. Common for smaller deals and faster turnaround. Include your handle or email on the invoice.

Wire transfer. For international deals or larger amounts. Wire details are similar to ACH but include SWIFT codes for international.

Pick one method and put it on the invoice. Multiple options is fine ("ACH preferred, PayPal accepted") but make sure the brand knows which one you actually want.

What to leave off

Equally important: what should not be on the invoice.

Your SSN. Never. SSN goes on the W-9 form, which is a separate document the brand requests for tax purposes. It does not go on the invoice itself. Anyone who has the invoice (whoever the AP person forwards it to, anyone who sees it in their inbox) does not need your SSN.

Bank account passwords or login info. Obvious but worth saying. You provide routing/account numbers, which are not secret (they are printed on every check). Anything else is not their business.

Long explanations of the work. Save the storytelling for your reports and recaps. The invoice is a transactional document. Short, clean line items, nothing more.

Personal commentary. "Loved working with you!" goes in the email body, not on the invoice. Keep the invoice professional and bare.

The creator-specific edge cases

A few situations that come up more often in creator work than in other freelancing:

The brand wants to pay you through an agency

Common when the brand hired an agency to coordinate the deal. You bill the agency, not the brand. The agency invoice and the brand invoice are separate (the agency invoices the brand on their side, you invoice the agency on yours). Make sure the contract is clear about which entity you are billing, then bill that one.

The deal includes content you have not posted yet

Some contracts pay 50 percent on signing and 50 percent on posting. In that case, you send two invoices. Each one references the relevant milestone: "50 percent deposit per contract clause 4 - $750" for the first, "Final payment upon content posting - $750" for the second. Two invoice numbers, two due dates, two emails.

This is the right way to handle it. If you try to lump it into one invoice that says "wait until posting then pay it all," you lose the leverage that a split-payment structure was supposed to give you. The whole point is getting paid for half before you deliver.

The deal got cancelled and you have a kill fee

If your contract had a kill fee clause (one of the things we covered in what to put in your brand deal contract), and the brand cancelled, you can still invoice for the kill fee amount. The line item references the clause: "Cancellation fee per contract clause 7 - $X." Brands sometimes "forget" they owe kill fees if you do not invoice for them. Invoice.

You are billing in a different currency

International deals are increasingly common. If the contract is in USD, bill in USD even if you are not in the US. If the contract is in another currency, bill in that currency, but include the USD equivalent as a reference for your own records.

The exchange rate trap to watch for: if you send a EUR invoice and the brand pays it 60 days later, the exchange rate may have moved. Whether you eat that difference or not depends on the contract. Most contracts pin the rate to the invoice date. Read yours.

What to do when the invoice is late

This is genuinely the most painful part of creator income. The deal is done, the work is delivered, the invoice is sent, and then... silence.

Net 30 means 30 days, but the unwritten reality is that many brands and agencies start their internal AP process on day 25 or 30, which means actual payment often shows up on day 35-45. That is normal-ish. Day 60 is concerning. Day 90 is a real problem.

The escalation that usually works:

Day 30-35 (just past due): A short, polite follow-up email. "Hi, just following up on invoice INV-005 which was due [date]. Could you confirm where it is in the AP process?" That alone resolves most late invoices, because the brand contact reaches out internally to AP and the payment moves.

Day 45-50: If no payment and no response, a firmer follow-up. CC the brand contact (the person who hired you) and the AP email. "Hi, following up again on invoice INV-005. The original due date was [date]. Please advise on the expected payment date." This raises the visibility.

Day 60+: At this point you have a real problem. Send a formal demand letter referencing the contract and a specific deadline ("payment expected by [date], failing which we will consider this a breach of contract"). For amounts under $1,000, you might decide it is not worth the time and write it off. For larger amounts, this is the point where a lawyer's letter starts to become economical.

The best defense against late payments, honestly, is contract terms that give you leverage upfront. A 50/50 split with the first half on signing means you are not waiting for the entire payment. A late-payment fee in the contract ("invoices not paid within 30 days of due date accrue 1.5% monthly interest") often does not get enforced, but it gives you negotiating ground when you escalate.

A simple invoice template

If you want to write your own, here is the structure. Put each section as its own block on the page:

Top of the page:

INVOICE
Invoice #: INV-001
Date: [today's date]
Due date: [today + Net 30 or whatever applies]

Your info (top-left or top-right):

[Your legal name or business name]
[Your address, or city/state]
[Your email]
[Your phone]

Bill to (other side from your info):

[Client legal name]
[Client billing address]
Attn: [AP contact, if you know them]

Line items (middle of page, table-style):

Description | Quantity | Rate | Amount
1 Instagram Reel, posted [date] | 1 | $1,500 | $1,500
3 Instagram Stories, posted [date] | 3 | $167 | $500

Totals (bottom-right of the line items):

Subtotal: $2,000
Tax: $0
Total Due: $2,000

Payment instructions (below totals):

Payment via ACH:
Bank: [Your bank]
Routing: [routing number]
Account: [account number]

Or PayPal: [your PayPal email]

Optional footer:

Please reference invoice number on all payment correspondence. Thank you.

That is a real invoice. Save it as a template in whatever tool you use (Google Docs, a PDF generator, or a dedicated invoicing tool), and reuse the structure for every deal.

One last thing

Writing your first invoice feels intimidating because it is the moment when "I made a thing for a brand" becomes "I am running a business." It is also the moment most creators realize that the creative work was the easy part, and the business side has its own learning curve.

That curve is shorter than it looks. By your fifth invoice you will not think about the format anymore. By your tenth you will have a system. By your hundredth you will be telling other creators what to put on theirs. The trick is sending the first one even when you are not sure you are doing it right.

You probably are doing it mostly right. Brands deal with hundreds of creator invoices and they have seen every level of formality and informality. As long as the core elements are there (who, who, what, how much, how to pay), the invoice will get paid. The rest is polish you can add over time.
