How to Track Brand Deal Income for Taxes

The first time a creator gets a real brand deal, the question is "how much?" The second time, it's "how do I get paid faster?" By the third or fourth deal, a new question shows up: "wait, what happens with this at tax time?" And then, usually around February or March of the following year, a 1099-NEC arrives in the mail and the panic sets in.

This is the post about that panic. Not how to file (talk to a CPA), but what creators commonly need to know about brand deal income from a tax-tracking perspective so the panic is smaller when it arrives. Most of the work is just keeping good records all year. The actual filing part is easier when the record-keeping part has already happened.

Why Tracking Matters More Than Filing

The single biggest difference between a creator who has a smooth tax season and one who has a stressful one isn't how good their accountant is. It's whether they tracked income and expenses during the year, or tried to reconstruct everything in March from memory and bank statements.

If you treat your creator work like a business (something we touched on in content creator vs influencer), you're a small business owner whether or not you've registered an LLC. Brand deals, sponsored posts, affiliate commissions, course sales, coaching, digital product revenue, and Patreon income all count as self-employment income in the US. Your platform might collect some of it on your behalf (Stripe Connect, for example), but the responsibility to track and report it stays with you.

Tracking isn't about being a perfectionist. It's about not paying more tax than you owe because you forgot a deduction, and not getting hit with a surprise bill because you didn't realize quarterly payments were a thing.

What the 1099-NEC Is and Isn't

If you've done brand deals, you've probably gotten one of these. A 1099-NEC is a form a US business sends to anyone they paid $600 or more in a tax year for non-employee work. The brand sends one copy to you and another to the IRS. It says "we paid this person this much, please make sure they report it on their taxes."

A few things creators commonly misunderstand about 1099s:

  • You owe tax on the income even if you don't get a 1099. The $600 threshold is when the brand is required to send one. If a brand paid you $500, you don't get a form, but you still owe tax on that $500. The IRS expects you to report all income, not just income they received a form about.
  • The 1099 amount reflects gross, not net. If a brand paid you $5,000 and you spent $1,200 on production costs (props, location, an editor), the 1099 still says $5,000. You'll deduct the $1,200 separately on Schedule C.
  • Payment apps may send different forms. If you received brand payments through PayPal Business or Venmo Business, they may issue a 1099-K instead. Personal Venmo and Cash App generally don't issue forms, but the income is still reportable.
  • Brands sometimes get the math wrong. Compare every 1099 to your own records. If a brand reported $4,200 but you actually received $3,800, you'll need documentation to dispute it.

The "I never got a 1099 so I don't have to report it" idea is a common myth and a quick way to attract IRS attention later. Track everything regardless of whether a form ever shows up.

How Self-Employment Tax Works (In Plain English)

This is the part that surprises most new creators. When you work a regular W-2 job, your employer withholds federal and state income tax from your paycheck and also pays half of your Social Security and Medicare tax (FICA). You pay the other half. You barely notice it because it's already been taken out.

When you're self-employed, nobody withholds anything. You owe:

  • Federal income tax on your business income, at whatever bracket your total income falls into
  • Self-employment tax of 15.3% on your net business income, which is both halves of Social Security and Medicare combined (since you're paying as both employer and employee)
  • State income tax if your state has one
  • Possibly city or local tax depending on where you live

The 15.3% self-employment tax is the part that catches people off guard. A creator who earned $30,000 in brand deals and assumed they'd pay "maybe 12% tax" can suddenly owe closer to 25-30% of that income once self-employment tax stacks on top of federal income tax. This is why "set aside 25-30% of every payment for taxes" is the rule of thumb most creator-economy CPAs repeat.

The 15.3% self-employment tax is the part that catches people off guard. Most new creators underestimate their tax bill by half.

Quarterly Estimated Taxes

The IRS doesn't want to wait until April to get paid. If you expect to owe more than $1,000 in tax for the year (which happens at roughly $5,000-$10,000 in net self-employment income for most creators), you're generally expected to pay quarterly estimated taxes during the year instead of one big check at filing time.

The deadlines (US) are typically:

  • April 15 (Q1: income from January through March)
  • June 15 (Q2: income from April and May)
  • September 15 (Q3: income from June through August)
  • January 15 of the following year (Q4: income from September through December)

If you skip these and just pay everything in April, you might owe an underpayment penalty on top of the tax itself. The penalty is usually small (a few percent), but it's avoidable. A CPA can help you figure out how much to send each quarter based on your projected income.

This is one of the genuinely complicated parts of creator finance and a good reason to talk to a professional once your income gets meaningful. The cost of a CPA is often less than the penalties and missed deductions they help you avoid.

Common Deductions Creators Discuss

The flip side of paying self-employment tax is that you get to deduct legitimate business expenses, which lowers your taxable income. Below are categories that come up often in creator-focused tax discussions. Whether any of these apply to your specific situation depends on how you actually use the items. A CPA can help you figure out what's defensible.

Equipment

Cameras, lenses, microphones, lighting, tripods, gimbals, computers, monitors, hard drives, and phones used for content production. Big-ticket items may need to be depreciated over multiple years rather than deducted all at once. Save receipts.

Software and subscriptions

Editing software, design tools, scheduling apps, analytics platforms, cloud storage, stock photo subscriptions, music licensing, and yes, creator-economy platforms like Ryxa. If it's used for the business, it usually qualifies.

Home office

If you have a space in your home used exclusively for the business (an actual room or clearly defined area, not "the kitchen table"), a portion of rent, utilities, internet, and home expenses may be deductible. The rules are specific and worth getting right with a CPA.

Production costs

Props, set design, location rentals, hair and makeup for shoots, wardrobe purchased specifically for a campaign (not everyday clothes), travel directly tied to a brand deal, and payments to editors, photographers, or other freelancers you hire.

Marketing and professional development

Ad spend, website costs, professional courses, conference tickets, books and training related to your craft, and professional association memberships.

Mileage and travel

Driving to shoots, meetings with brands, conferences, and other business-related trips. The IRS sets a per-mile rate each year. Keep a log if you plan to claim this.

Phone and internet

Usually the business-use percentage of your monthly bills. If you use your phone 60% for creator work and 40% personal, you may deduct 60% of the bill.

The rule of thumb most CPAs repeat: if it's ordinary (common for your line of work) and necessary (helpful for your business), it's likely deductible. Personal expenses dressed up as business expenses are not, and "creator" is a category the IRS is increasingly familiar with.

Records You Should Keep

For each brand deal and other source of business income, the records that matter:

  • Who paid you (the brand or agency, with their tax ID if available)
  • How much (gross amount, before any platform fees)
  • When (date you received payment, not when the deal was signed)
  • What for (sponsored post, UGC video, affiliate commission, course sale)
  • How they paid (bank transfer, PayPal, Venmo, check)
  • Any contract or signed agreement
  • Any 1099 they sent at year-end

For expenses, save receipts (digital is fine) and tag each one to a category. Bank and credit card statements alone aren't usually sufficient. You want the actual receipt or invoice. Most CPAs recommend keeping these records for at least 7 years.

The fastest way to fail at this is to do it once a year. The income comes in monthly, the receipts come in daily, and trying to reconstruct a full year from memory in March is where mistakes (and missed deductions) happen.

The Workflow That Actually Works

Most creators who keep clean books do some version of this:

  • Log every brand deal when it lands. Not when the campaign concludes. When the brand confirms the deal. Capture the amount, deliverables, deadline, and payment terms.
  • Log every payment when it hits. When the money is actually in your account, mark the deal as paid with the date. This is the date that matters for tax purposes.
  • Categorize expenses weekly, not yearly. Spend 10 minutes once a week dragging receipts into a folder or app and tagging them. It's a tiny task weekly, a horrible task annually.
  • Set aside taxes as you get paid, not at year-end. A separate savings account labeled "taxes" where 25-30% of every brand payment goes the day you receive it. That way the money is there when quarterly payments are due and you're never reaching for funds you've already spent.
  • Reconcile monthly. Match your income log to your bank deposits. Catch any deals where the brand short-paid or paid the wrong amount while it's still close enough to fix.

LLC, Sole Proprietor, S-Corp: A Brief Note

One of the most common questions creators ask is "should I form an LLC?" The honest answer is "talk to a CPA who knows your specific income and state." But here's roughly what people are choosing between:

  • Sole proprietor. The default. If you make money from creator work and haven't filed any business paperwork, you're already a sole proprietor. Income flows to your personal tax return on Schedule C. Cheap and simple.
  • Single-member LLC. Files the same as a sole proprietor by default (Schedule C). The reason creators form LLCs is usually liability protection, not tax savings. State filing fees vary widely.
  • S-Corp election. An LLC can elect to be taxed as an S-Corp, which can save on self-employment tax once income is high enough (often $70,000+ in net profit). Comes with extra paperwork and the requirement to pay yourself a "reasonable salary." Almost always worth talking to a CPA before doing this.

The takeaway: don't rush to form an LLC because you saw a TikTok about it. The right structure depends on income level, state, what kind of work you do, and what risks you're trying to protect against. A 30-minute consultation with a CPA is usually enough to figure it out for your situation.

What "Set Aside 25-30%" Means

This is the most-repeated piece of creator tax advice and worth unpacking. The 25-30% figure is a rough estimate of what most US creators owe on net self-employment income once you add federal income tax, self-employment tax, and state tax together. It's not your final tax bill. It's a buffer to make sure the money is there when you owe it.

For lower earners, the real number might be closer to 20%. For high earners in high-tax states like California or New York, it can be closer to 35-40%. The point of setting aside a generic 25-30% is that you'd rather over-save than be short. Whatever isn't owed at tax time is just savings that became available again.

Some creators get more specific by running their numbers through a self-employment tax calculator each quarter and adjusting the set-aside percentage based on their actual situation. Either approach is better than not setting aside anything.

When to Get a CPA Involved

If any of these are true, it's probably time:

  • You're earning more than ~$20,000 a year from creator work
  • You have brand deals across multiple states or countries
  • You're considering an LLC or S-Corp
  • You've never paid quarterly taxes and the IRS sent you a letter
  • You're filing for the first time as a self-employed creator
  • You sold a course or digital product internationally (sales tax/VAT gets complicated)
  • You hired someone (now you have payroll concerns)

A good CPA for creators understands the industry, doesn't blink at deductions like "ring light" or "stock music subscription," and will often save you more than they cost. Look for someone who has at least a handful of self-employed creators or freelancers as clients. The first call is usually free.

The Realistic Bottom Line

You don't need to become an accountant to handle creator income at tax time. You need to track what comes in, track what goes out, set aside a percentage as you go, keep your receipts, and hand the records to a professional when it gets complex enough to warrant one.

The "set aside 25-30%, track everything, talk to a CPA when income is meaningful" workflow handles the vast majority of creator situations. The work is in the tracking, not the filing. Get the tracking right, and tax season stops being scary.

Brand deals are a great income stream (especially once you know how to price them properly), but they come with the responsibility of treating yourself like a business. Track from day one. Future-you will be grateful.
