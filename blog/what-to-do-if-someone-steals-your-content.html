What to Do If Someone Steals Your Content

Ryxa 📅 May 2026 ⏱ 11 min read

You are scrolling, and there it is. Your photo, your video, your Reel, the one that took you four hours to shoot and edit, posted by an account that is not yours. Sometimes the caption is copied word for word. Sometimes they cropped out your watermark. Sometimes the account has more followers than you do, and it stings even more.

Content theft is one of the most common and most demoralizing things creators deal with. The good news is that you have real tools, and the platforms have real processes for exactly this. The bad news is that nobody explains those processes clearly, so most creators either rage-comment under the stolen post (which does nothing) or give up. This guide is the clear version: what content theft actually is, how to report it on each platform, how a DMCA takedown really works, and how to make your work harder to steal in the first place.

First, Figure Out What Kind of "Theft" This Is

Not everything that feels like theft is a violation a platform will act on. Sorting this out first saves you from filing reports that go nowhere.

  • Direct reposting of your exact file. Someone downloaded your photo or video and re-uploaded it as their own. This is the clearest case. Platforms act on this, and it is what DMCA was built for.
  • Reposting with your watermark removed or cropped. Still a clear violation, and the removal of your mark can actually strengthen your case, because it shows intent.
  • A near-identical copy of your idea. Someone recreated your video concept, your recipe format, your transition, your joke structure. This is the frustrating gray zone. Copyright protects your specific expression, the actual footage and words, not the underlying idea or format. A shot-for-shot recreation may still be actionable, but "they copied my concept" usually is not.
  • Someone using your content in an ad or for commercial gain. This is the most serious version, and the one most worth pursuing hard, especially if a business is profiting from your work.
  • A "repost" account that credits you. Annoying to some creators, genuinely fine to others. If they tagged you and you did not give permission, you can still ask for removal, but decide whether it is worth your energy. Some creators treat tagged reposts as free reach.

Be honest with yourself about which category you are in. The clearest, fastest wins are direct re-uploads of your actual files. The idea-copying cases are real and painful, but they are much harder to enforce, and knowing that upfront keeps you from burning hours on a report that will be denied.

Step One, Always: Document Everything

Before you do anything else, before you comment, before you DM the thief, before you file a report, gather evidence. If the thief notices you and deletes the post, your case can evaporate. Capture it while it exists.

  • Screenshot the stolen post, including the account name, the caption, the date, and the like and view counts.
  • Copy the direct URL to the infringing post. You will need it for every report you file.
  • Locate your original. Find your own post, note its publication date, and screenshot it. The fact that yours is older is your proof of being first.
  • Save your source files. The raw photo, the project file, the unedited footage. Original files with their metadata are some of the strongest possible evidence of authorship. This is one more reason to keep organized backups of your work.

The single most common reason a content-theft complaint fails is that the creator did not document it before the post disappeared or before they tipped off the thief. Treat evidence-gathering as step zero, before any contact and before any public comment.

The Platform-by-Platform Reporting Routes

Every major platform has a copyright reporting flow. They are not always easy to find, but they exist and they work.

Instagram

Instagram has a dedicated copyright report form in its help center, separate from the regular "report post" button. The regular report button is for things like spam and harassment, and using it for copyright often goes nowhere. Search for "Instagram copyright report form," use the official help-center result, and submit the infringing URL, a link to or description of your original, and your contact information. As the rights holder you can file directly.

TikTok

TikTok has both an in-app report option and a dedicated online Intellectual Property Infringement form. For a clear case of your video being re-uploaded, the in-app report (tap the share arrow, then Report, then choose the intellectual-property option) is quick. For anything more involved, or if the in-app report is ignored, use TikTok's web copyright form, which is the more formal channel.

YouTube

YouTube has the most developed system, because it has dealt with this the longest. You can submit a copyright takedown request through the form in YouTube's help center or directly from the video's options. YouTube also has a "report" flow, but for actual copyright you want the copyright-specific complaint, which is a legal request and is treated as one.

Other platforms and websites

If your content was stolen onto a website rather than a social platform, you file the DMCA notice with the site's host or the site owner directly. More on that next.

How a DMCA Takedown Actually Works

"DMCA" gets thrown around a lot, and most creators are fuzzy on what it actually is. Here is the plain version.

The DMCA, the Digital Millennium Copyright Act, is a United States law. One part of it gives online platforms legal protection from being sued over user-uploaded content, on the condition that they promptly remove infringing content when a valid complaint comes in. That condition is why every major platform has a takedown process. They are legally motivated to act on valid notices.

A DMCA takedown notice is a formal request that generally needs to include:

  1. Identification of your original work, the content you created and own.
  2. Identification of the infringing material, the specific URL of the stolen post.
  3. Your contact information, so the platform and the alleged infringer can reach you.
  4. A good-faith statement that you believe the use is not authorized by you, the copyright owner, or the law.
  5. A statement, under penalty of perjury, that the information is accurate and that you are the rights holder or authorized to act for them.
  6. Your signature, which can be electronic.

When you use a platform's official copyright form, it is essentially walking you through a DMCA notice. You do not usually need a lawyer to file one for a straightforward case of your own work being copied.

Two things to understand about the process. First, the thief can file a counter-notice. If they claim the takedown was wrong, the content can be restored unless you escalate, which for most creators is not worth it for a single stolen post. Second, filing a false DMCA notice has legal consequences. That perjury statement is real. Only file over content you genuinely created and own. Do not use DMCA as a weapon in a personal feud.

A DMCA notice is not a lawsuit and it is not a threat. It is a standard, expected request that platforms are legally built to act on. Treat it as paperwork, not war.

Should You Contact the Person Directly?

Sometimes a polite direct message resolves it faster than any form. A surprising number of reposters are not malicious, they are careless, and a simple "hey, that is my video, please take it down or add credit" works.

But weigh it carefully:

  • Document first, always. Never message the thief before you have screenshots and URLs. Tipping them off can mean they delete and re-post under a new account, and your evidence is gone.
  • Keep it factual and unemotional. "This is my content, posted originally on [date]. Please remove it within 48 hours or I will file a copyright report." Calm and specific outperforms angry every time.
  • Do not rally your audience to attack them. Siccing your followers on someone can rebound on you, can count as harassment, and can turn a simple copyright matter into a messy public fight. Use the official process instead.
  • If it is a business profiting from your work, skip straight to the formal route. A company using your content commercially is unlikely to be moved by a friendly DM, and the formal complaint carries more weight.

How to Make Your Content Harder to Steal

You cannot make theft impossible, but you can make it less appealing and easier to prove when it happens.

  • Watermark in a way that is hard to crop. A tiny logo in the corner gets cropped out in seconds. A subtle mark placed where cropping it would damage the content, or your handle worked naturally into the video itself, survives longer and proves origin.
  • Keep your original files and project files, organized. Raw footage, edit files, and originals with intact metadata are powerful proof of authorship. A creator who can produce the source file wins almost every dispute. Keep real backups of your work, not just whatever is sitting in the app.
  • Post to a channel you own, not only to rented platforms. When your work also lives somewhere you control, with a clear publish date under your name, you have a permanent, timestamped record of being first. A link in bio page or your own creator site gives you that anchor, independent of any single platform's memory.
  • Build the habit of dating and signing your posts. Consistent watermarking and consistent posting create a paper trail that makes "I posted this first" trivial to demonstrate.
  • Sell, do not just display, where it matters. If your content is the product, putting the high-value version behind a real purchase rather than free on a feed changes the theft calculus entirely. We touched on this dynamic in can I sell courses on Instagram, and the principle applies to any premium work: a free public file is the easiest thing in the world to copy.

Keep It in Perspective

One hard truth: if you make good content, some of it will get copied. Reposting is endemic to social platforms, and you cannot file your way to zero. Spending your life patrolling for thieves is its own kind of loss. We have written before about how to deal with creator burnout, and an obsessive theft-hunting habit is a real contributor to it.

So calibrate. The cases genuinely worth your time and a formal complaint: a business profiting from your work, a large account stealing your best-performing content wholesale, anything misrepresenting who you are or what you stand for, and anything costing you real money or partnerships. The cases usually not worth the spiral: a small account that reposted one thing with credit, a vague format similarity, a copy that nobody is really seeing. Win the fights that matter, let the small stuff go, and put the saved energy back into making the next thing.

The Bottom Line

If your content was stolen: document it before you do anything else, screenshots, URLs, your original's date, your source files. Figure out which kind of theft it is, because direct re-uploads are the winnable cases. File through the platform's official copyright form, not the generic report button, which is effectively filing a DMCA notice. Consider a calm, factual direct message for careless reposters, but go straight to the formal route for businesses and bad actors.

Then make the next theft harder: watermark smartly, keep organized backups and originals, and make sure your work also lives on a channel you own so you always have proof of being first. You will never stop theft entirely. But you can make it rare, make it provable, and make sure it never takes more from your business than a little time.
