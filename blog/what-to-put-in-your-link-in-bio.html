Link in Bio

What Should I Put in My Link in Bio?

Most link-in-bio pages are graveyards. A blank page with three social icons, a half-finished email signup, and a single Linktree-style button to "Latest YouTube Video" that hasn't been updated since last summer.

If you're staring at the editor wondering what actually goes here, this is the working list. Fifteen things that earn their spot, in rough order of conversion impact, with the reasoning behind each one. The post ends with the mistakes that quietly kill performance regardless of what you put there.

The Hierarchy

Before the list, the principle: your link in bio is not a directory. It's a funnel. The job is to move someone from passive viewer to one specific next action that benefits both of you. Every link competes for attention, and links below the fold often get under 5% of the clicks of the top link.

So order matters more than count. Three sharp links beat twelve scattered ones. Put your most valuable destination at the top, every time, even if you have to repeat it elsewhere on the page.

Three sharp links beat twelve scattered ones.

1. The "What I'm Selling Right Now" Link

Top of the page. No exceptions.

If you have one offer, course, coaching service, digital product, or affiliate that earns you money, it goes first. Not third. Not "after my latest YouTube video." First.

This is the link that justifies the entire page existing. If you're not comfortable putting your offer at the top, that's a sign you don't believe in it strongly enough to charge for it. Either fix the offer or pick a different lead link, but don't bury revenue.

2. Newsletter or Email Signup

The most underrated link on every bio page.

Followers belong to the platform. Email subscribers belong to you. Every algorithm change, account ban, or platform exodus erases your reach overnight. Your email list does not.

Don't bury this in a footer. A direct email signup form, embedded right in your bio page (not just a link to a separate landing page), gets dramatically better conversion than a button that says "Join my newsletter." Make the friction zero.

3. A Free Resource That Demonstrates Value

The Trojan horse for your real offer.

A free guide, template, checklist, or short video that solves a specific problem your audience has. Gated behind email signup, ideally. Three benefits in one: builds your list, proves you can deliver value, primes them to buy when you eventually pitch a paid offer.

The trick is making it feel like something you'd charge for. "Top 5 Tips" PDFs that look like SEO blog posts won't move the needle. A genuinely useful spreadsheet, template, or 10-page guide will.

4. Your Media Kit (If You Take Brand Deals)

How brands find you, qualify you, and decide to reach out.

If brand collaborations are part of your income, your bio link should make it impossible to miss. A clean media kit with audience stats, past partnerships, rate ranges, and a contact path turns "interested brand" into "brand that emails you" without you doing anything.

Most creators hide their media kit in a Google Drive link buried in their email signature. Putting it directly in your bio page signals you're ready for paid work and shortens the brand's evaluation cycle from days to minutes.

5. Booking Link for 1:1 Calls or Sessions

If you offer coaching, consulting, or any kind of paid time, this needs to be one click away.

Embedded calendar booking is dramatically better than "DM for rates" or "Email me to book." Most people who would have booked don't make the second jump. They see the friction and bounce. (We compared the best booking tools for creators if you're trying to pick one.)

If you're nervous about always-on availability, set tight booking windows (two slots a week, two weeks out). A booking link doesn't have to mean infinite availability. It just has to remove the back-and-forth.

6. Latest Featured Content (Pinned, Not Auto-Updated)

One specific piece of content, hand-selected.

Not "Latest YouTube Video" auto-pulled from RSS. A specific video, post, or article you've decided is your best entry point for someone who just discovered you. Pin it. Update it once a month or quarter, not weekly.

The reason auto-updating links underperform is they sometimes show your weakest recent content. Hand-selected content showcases your best work, which is what a first-time visitor needs to see.

7. Affiliate Links to Products You Actually Use

Curated, specific, with one-line context.

If you're a fitness creator, link to the specific protein powder you actually drink, not a generic Amazon storefront. If you're a tech creator, link to the exact mic, camera, or software you use. The specificity is what converts. Generic Amazon shop links get under 1% click-through. Specific "this is what I use to film" links can hit 8-15%.

One line of context per link. "The mic I've used for 200 podcast episodes." Not just "My equipment."

8. Your Most Recent Launch or Drop

If you have something new, push it hard for a window.

New course launching? New product drop? New collab dropping next Friday? Put it at the top, with a date, for the duration of the launch. Then take it down. Permanent "Pre-Order!" links that have been there for six months train your audience to ignore launch links forever.

Urgency only works if it's actual urgency. Use it sparingly so it still works when you need it.

9. A "Work With Me" Page

For creators who do multiple types of paid work.

If you do brand deals AND coaching AND speaking AND consulting, you don't need four separate links. One "Work with me" page that explains all your offerings, with rates and contact info, beats fragmenting your bio across four buttons that say similar things.

The principle: if you can consolidate two links into one, do it. Cognitive load is a conversion killer.

10. Discord, Community, or Group Chat Invite

For creators with an engaged tribe.

Free Discord servers, Telegram groups, paid communities. If you have one, link it. Community is some of the stickiest engagement available, and it converts to other offerings (courses, paid memberships) better than almost any other channel.

Skip this if your community is small or inactive. An empty Discord with five members is worse than no Discord link at all.

11. Podcast or Show Link

If you have one, with a "listen on your platform" picker.

Don't pick "Listen on Spotify" only. Some of your audience is on Apple, some on Pocket Casts, some watching the YouTube version. A single link that lets them choose converts substantially better than forcing one platform.

Most link-in-bio tools support a podcast picker block specifically for this. Use it.

12. A Specific Social Account You Want to Grow

Push the platform you're trying to build, not the one you came from.

If most of your audience finds you on Instagram and you're trying to grow your TikTok or YouTube, your bio link should push them there. Cross-platform redirection is one of the most underused growth tactics. People who follow you on Instagram and TikTok are dramatically more loyal than single-platform followers.

The reverse is also true. If you came from YouTube and are trying to grow Instagram, your YouTube description and link in bio should push to Instagram. Stop sending traffic to platforms where you already have it.

13. Speaking or Press Inquiries Form

For creators who get asked to speak, podcast guest, or be quoted in articles.

A simple form that captures the inquiry type, audience size, date, topic, and budget. Saves you the email back-and-forth of "What's the audience? What's the topic? When?"

Skip if this isn't a regular flow for you. Adding it preemptively for an "if I get asked someday" scenario clutters the page.

14. Charity or Cause You Support

If you actively support one and it's part of your identity, fine.

One link, one cause, with a one-line explanation. Don't add it because it seems like a good idea or because you think it'll make you look better. Your audience can tell the difference between a creator who genuinely cares about a cause and one who's including it for image management. The second is worse than nothing.

15. A "Past Work" Portfolio Link

For creators with a body of polished work to showcase.

Photographers, videographers, designers, writers. Your bio link is one of the highest-traffic pages a potential client might see, and a portfolio link converts visitors who landed on your social profile into people who can see your professional work in 10 seconds.

Skip if your "best work" is your social content itself. In that case, your social profile IS the portfolio, and a separate link is redundant.

The Mistakes That Quietly Kill Performance

You can pick the right links and still tank your conversion if you make any of these errors. Some are obvious, some aren't.

Too many links

The single biggest mistake. More links does not mean more clicks. It means each link gets fewer clicks. Studies of link-in-bio behavior consistently show clicks concentrated in the top 1-3 links, and dropping off sharply after. If you have 15 links, links 8-15 are getting almost zero traffic and are diluting attention from your top 3.

Aim for 5-7 links total at most. If you have more than that, ruthlessly prune.

Generic button copy

"Click here." "My website." "Latest video." Buttons named like this get fewer clicks than buttons with specific, value-driven copy. "Get my free Notion template" beats "My templates" by a wide margin. "Book a 30-min strategy call" beats "Coaching" by a wider margin. The cost is just thinking carefully about each label.

Outdated or seasonal links left up year-round

Black Friday sale link still up in March. "New launch coming soon!" that's been there for nine months. Holiday-themed buttons months after the holiday. These signal abandonment, and abandoned-feeling pages convert worse across all links, not just the stale ones.

No call to action below your offers

People read top-down and stop reading when they hit a wall of similar buttons. Including small section dividers ("Free stuff" / "Work with me" / "Recent content") with brief copy between them dramatically improves engagement on lower buttons. Most link-in-bio tools support section headers. Use them.

Hiding your most important link below social media buttons

Many creators put their Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube icons at the very top, then their offers below. This is backwards. Anyone who's seeing your link in bio already found you on social. Sending them BACK to social is wasting the click. Your owned destinations (newsletter, products, coaching) should be top-of-page. Social profiles are exit ramps, not entrances.

Not testing or measuring

If your link-in-bio tool reports clicks per link, look at the data monthly. Drop the bottom-performing two links. Move the top-performing one higher if it isn't already there. The creators with high-converting bios aren't smarter, they're just iterating on real data instead of guessing.

A note on order: The list above is loosely ordered by impact, but your specific situation matters more than any general ranking. A creator who makes 80% of income from brand deals should put their media kit at #1. A creator who sells courses should lead with the course (here's how to price one if that's the offer). The principle is universal (order by your actual revenue priorities), but the specific order is yours.

The Quick Self-Audit

Open your link in bio right now and ask:

  • Is the top link the one I most want a stranger to click? If not, move it.
  • Are there links that haven't been clicked in 30 days? Delete them.
  • Is there at least one direct path to my email list? If not, add one before you do anything else.
  • Are my button labels specific or generic? Rewrite the generic ones.
  • If I were a stranger, would I understand what this person does within five seconds? If not, that's the real fix.

The right link in bio doesn't try to do everything. It picks two or three things you actually want a stranger to do, and makes those impossibly easy to do. Everything else is noise.
