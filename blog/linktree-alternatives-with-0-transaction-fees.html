Linktree Alternatives with 0% Transaction Fees

Ryxa Team • May 2026 • 8 min read

Linktree's link-in-bio page is fine. The free tier is fine. The custom themes are fine. The button styles are fine.

What's not fine, if you actually sell anything, is what they take when a customer pays you through that link.

This post is for the creators who landed on Linktree thinking it was just a place to put their links, and then noticed something weird about their payouts.

The Quiet Linktree Fee You Probably Missed

Linktree advertises itself as a free link-in-bio tool. And the link-in-bio part is free. Their core feature, the page where you list your stuff, costs nothing on the basic tier.

What costs money is selling through Linktree. When a customer buys a digital product, tip, or paid subscription through your Linktree page, Linktree takes a percentage of every sale on top of the standard payment processor fees. The exact percentage varies by tier and product type, but the principle is the same one most creator platforms use: a cut of every dollar that flows through their tool.

The framing the platforms prefer is "we only get paid when you get paid." Sounds aligned. The reality is: as you grow, their take grows linearly while their cost to serve you barely changes. You're essentially paying rent on your own audience.

The Math on a Creator-Sized Business

Take a creator selling a $25 digital product through their link in bio. Ten sales a week. That's $1,000 a month in gross revenue.

What a percentage fee actually costs

$1,000 monthly revenue

5% platform fee = $50/month

$600/year on one product

And that's not counting payment processor fees (typically 2.9% + 30¢ per transaction, which any platform passes through). It also assumes you stay at $1,000/month forever. Scale to $5,000/month and the cut becomes $3,000/year. $10,000/month, $6,000/year.

Compare that to a flat-rate platform that charges $20/month regardless of how much you sell. At $1,000 in monthly revenue, you've paid $240 for the year. At $10,000/month, still $240 for the year. The percentage you give up shrinks as you grow instead of growing as you grow.

You're not paying for a link-in-bio page. You're paying a tax on selling through your audience.

Why People Stay on Linktree Anyway

Three reasons creators don't switch even when the math is bad:

The fee is invisible at deposit time.

The platform's cut is taken before the money hits your account. You see what landed in your bank, not what you would have had. Multiply across 100 transactions and you never see the cumulative number.

"Free for the basic tier" obscures the real cost.

The headline price is zero. The cost only shows up when you actually use the platform for what most creators eventually want it for: making money. By the time you're earning enough for the percentage to matter, you've already invested in the platform.

Switching feels harder than it is.

You've put your Linktree URL in your Instagram bio, your TikTok, your YouTube channel description, your business cards. Switching means updating those, plus rebuilding your link-in-bio page on a new platform. Realistically: 30 minutes of copy-paste work. But it FEELS like a project, so it gets put off.

What to Look For in a Linktree Alternative

If you sell anything through your link in bio, these matter:

  • Zero platform percentage on transactions. The platform should make money on subscription, not on every sale.
  • Direct integration with the major payment processors. Stripe and PayPal handle the actual money movement. The platform shouldn't be a middleman taking another cut.
  • Built-in product types. Courses, coaching, digital products, paid newsletters. Not just generic "links" you have to wire up to external services.
  • Custom domain support. Most platforms gate this behind a paid tier. It's table-stakes for a creator brand.
  • Owned email capture. The platform shouldn't make it hard to export your subscribers if you decide to leave.
  • Reasonable subscription pricing. A flat $10-30/month is the sweet spot. Anything higher needs to deliver dramatically more value.

Why "0% Transaction Fees" Is the Right Filter

You'll see plenty of "Linktree alternatives" lists with 15+ options. Most of them charge a percentage of sales, just maybe a smaller one than Linktree. Going from 5% to 3% is fine. Going from 5% to 0% is structurally different.

Here's why the structural difference matters more than the percentage difference:

Percentage fees scale with your success. They take a permanent slice of every dollar you ever earn through the platform. As you grow, their take grows. You can't outgrow them.

Flat fees don't scale with success. A subscription is a subscription. Whether you make $500 or $50,000 in a month, the platform charges the same. The effective fee percentage shrinks as you grow.

For a creator just starting out, a 5% fee on $200 in monthly sales is $10. Painless. For an established creator at $20,000 in monthly sales, that same 5% is $1,000 a month. That's a salary line item.

Worth naming: payment processor fees (Stripe at ~2.9% + 30¢ per transaction) are unavoidable on any platform. Those go to Stripe, not the link-in-bio platform. The question this post focuses on is whether your link-in-bio platform also takes a cut on top of Stripe's.

What Switching Actually Looks Like

The migration from any link-in-bio tool to another is mostly mechanical:

  • Sign up for the new platform. Fifteen minutes including connecting your Stripe account.
  • Recreate your link list. Most pages have 5-7 links. Copy-paste. Twenty minutes.
  • Update your social media bios. Replace the old URL with the new one. Five minutes per platform you're on.
  • Test the flow. Click through your own link in bio in incognito. Make sure everything works.
  • Forward the old URL. Some platforms let you redirect. If not, just take the old page down so visitors don't see a stale version.

Total time investment: under an hour. Total savings: depends on how much you sell, but for a creator doing $2,000/month or more in sales, switching pays for itself in the first month.

The Honest Question

If your link in bio is purely a directory (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube channel, podcast) and you don't sell anything through it, Linktree is fine. The free tier is genuinely free for that use case.

If you sell ANYTHING through your link in bio (courses, coaching, products, paid newsletters, even tip jars), the math changes. The percentage Linktree takes goes from "annoying detail" to "real expense" the moment you start having actual sales.

Calculate what you paid in transaction fees last year. Look at the actual dollars, not the percentage. If that number would matter to you in any other context, it should matter here too.

The Bottom Line

Linktree built a great link-in-bio product. Their pricing model just doesn't favor creators who sell. As you grow, the percentage they take grows with you, and the only way to stop paying is to leave.

The structural alternative is a platform that charges a flat subscription and 0% on transactions. You pay the same whether you make $100 a month or $100,000. The platform's incentive is to keep you happy enough to subscribe, not to take a cut of everything you earn.

For most creators selling through their link in bio, that's a better deal. The math is the math.
