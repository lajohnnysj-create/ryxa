Do I Need a Website If I Have Instagram?

Short answer: most creators don't need a traditional website anymore. What they need is a single page that does what a website used to do, without the work of building one.

That's not a sales pitch. It's the honest read on how Instagram changed where creator businesses actually run. The longer answer is below, with the cases where a website still makes sense and the cases where you're spending money and time on something your audience won't visit.

What a Website Used to Do

A website used to be a creator's home base for three things:

  • Discoverability: a place Google could index so people searching for you (or your topic) could find you
  • Credibility: proof you were a real business, not just a social media account
  • Sales: a place to actually sell something, take payment, and deliver a digital product

Five years ago, all three required a website. Today, only one of them really still does, and only for some creators.

What's Changed

Discoverability is mostly happening on social platforms now. People search for "best running shoes for flat feet" on TikTok and Instagram before they Google it. SEO still matters in some niches (we'll get to that), but for most creator categories, your reach is on the platforms, not on a personal website that almost nobody visits.

Credibility shifted too. A clean Instagram profile with consistent content and engaged followers is more credible to most audiences than a slick personal website. Brands evaluating you for sponsorships look at your social profiles first, then a media kit. A "website" rarely enters the conversation. (More on the media kit angle below.)

Sales is the interesting one. You used to need a website with a payment processor, a digital delivery system, and a customer service email to sell anything. Today, link-in-bio platforms handle that entire stack. Your audience taps a link, picks a product or books a session, pays, and gets what they bought, all without you owning a domain or paying a developer.

You don't need a website to run a creator business. You need a place where the transaction happens.

When You Still Need a Website

There are real cases where a traditional website earns its keep. If you're in one of these, build it. If you're not, skip it.

1. SEO is your primary growth channel.

If your business is built on people Googling something and finding you, you need a website. Bloggers, recipe creators, finance writers, and anyone whose audience reads long-form content via search results all fall here. Instagram won't rank in Google for "best protein pancake recipe" the way a blog post will. A website is the SEO real estate.

2. You sell physical products with a real catalog.

If you have 40 SKUs of skincare or apparel, with sizes and variants, you need an e-commerce site (Shopify, WooCommerce, etc.). Link-in-bio tools handle a handful of products well. They're not built for managing inventory across hundreds of items.

3. You're building a brand bigger than yourself.

If your goal is to build a company with employees, a press page, careers, B2B sales, or a complex content library, a website is the right home for that. This is less "creator" and more "business," but the line blurs at scale.

4. You need a custom domain for trust signals.

Some niches (legal, medical, financial) require a level of professionalism that a link-in-bio page on a shared domain doesn't carry. If your audience expects to see yourname.com in their browser, give them yourname.com. (Many link-in-bio platforms support custom domains, which can split the difference.)

When You Don't Need a Website

For most creators making most of their income from Instagram, TikTok, or YouTube, the answer is no. Specifically:

  • You earn from brand deals, sponsored content, or affiliate links
  • You sell digital products (presets, templates, ebooks, guides) one or two at a time, not a 50-product catalog
  • You take 1:1 bookings (coaching, consulting, calls) and need a way for clients to schedule and pay
  • You sell access to a course or membership
  • You collect emails for a newsletter

Every one of these can be handled on a single link-in-bio page. The page acts as your home base. Your social profiles drive traffic to it. The page handles transactions, bookings, and product delivery.

If you've been told you need a website to be "professional," consider where the advice came from. Web designers say you need a website. Hosting companies say you need a website. The actual numbers on creator revenue suggest most of it flows through social profiles plus a single landing page, not a multi-page site.

Where the Confusion Comes From

"Do I need a website?" is really three questions stacked on top of each other. Untangling them helps:

  • Do I need a place where people can find my links and offers? Yes, always.
  • Do I need a place where transactions happen? Yes, if you're selling anything.
  • Do I need a multi-page site on a custom domain that I built or paid to have built? Usually no, unless you're in one of the cases above.

The first two questions are answered by a good link-in-bio page. The third question is the one that costs money and time, and it's the one most creators don't need answered.

The Practical Setup

If you've decided you don't need a full website (yet), here's the minimum viable setup:

  • An Instagram profile with a clear bio, a single CTA, and one link
  • That link points to a link-in-bio page
  • The page has your top 3-5 offers, ranked by what makes you money
  • The page handles bookings, digital product sales, or affiliate links inline
  • You have an email capture somewhere on the page

That's it. You can add a custom domain to the link-in-bio page if you want the look of a "real website." For most creators, this setup outperforms a traditional website because it's optimized for the only traffic source that actually drives revenue: people clicking through from social.

The Honest Bottom Line

"Do I need a website?" is the wrong question. The right question is: "Where do I want my audience to land, and what do I want them to do there?"

If the answer is "buy a product, book a call, or join my email list," you don't need a website. You need a single page that handles those three things well. If the answer is "find my long-form content via Google," you do need a website. If the answer is "browse 80 SKUs of a product line I run," you need an e-commerce site.

Pick the tool that matches the job. Don't build a website because someone told you that's what real creators do. Real creators are everywhere on the internet, and most of their income flows through one Instagram bio link.
