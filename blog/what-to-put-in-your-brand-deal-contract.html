What to Put in Your Brand Deal Contract

Educational content only. This article explains common clauses and concepts that show up in creator brand deal contracts. It is not legal advice. Contract law varies by country, state, and the specific terms of any agreement, and the right contract for one deal may be wrong for another. Before signing anything that involves real money, exclusivity, or content rights, talk to a qualified attorney. The information here is current as of 2026 but specific laws and norms shift.

The first brand deal most creators sign is also the first contract most creators read. It usually shows up as a PDF in an email from a brand manager, with a friendly note that says "let me know if you have any questions!" The creator scrolls through it, doesn't really understand most of it, doesn't want to look unprofessional by asking, signs, and learns the hard way over the next few weeks what they agreed to.

That pattern repeats a lot. Not because creators are careless, but because nobody teaches creators to read contracts the way creators are taught to make content. The contracts also tend to be written in language that sounds intimidating, even when the underlying concepts are not actually complicated.

This post is the plain-English version. We will walk through the clauses that show up in almost every brand deal contract, what each one actually means, what is reasonable to accept, and what is worth pushing back on. The goal is not to make you a lawyer. The goal is to help you read the next contract that lands in your inbox and recognize what you are looking at.

The clauses you will see in almost every brand deal contract

A brand deal contract sounds complicated because it is wrapped in legal phrasing, but it is really answering a small set of questions. Who is doing what, by when, for how much, where it can be used, and what happens if something goes wrong. Every other clause is a variation on those.

1. Scope of work and deliverables

This is the part that says exactly what you are making. One Instagram Reel, two Stories, one TikTok post. The number of pieces, on which platforms, in which format.

The thing to watch for here is vagueness. "Social media content" is not a deliverable. "One Reel and three Stories" is a deliverable. If the scope is fuzzy, a brand can later argue you owe them more than you intended. If the scope is specific, you both know exactly when you are done. Push for specifics if the contract doesn't have them.

Also watch for hidden deliverables buried elsewhere in the contract. Sometimes the scope section says "one Reel," and then a later section says "creator will also provide three approved still photos for brand use." That is four deliverables, not one. Read the whole thing before you agree to the price.

2. Timeline and posting dates

When you submit drafts, when revisions are due, when the content goes live. Most contracts specify a posting window (often a specific date or a 48-hour window). If you post outside that window without permission, the brand can technically claim you breached the contract.

The piece that catches creators by surprise: revision rounds. A contract might say "up to two rounds of revisions." If a brand sends a third round of feedback, you are within your rights to push back. If the contract doesn't cap revisions at all, that is a problem worth fixing before signing. Endless revisions on a one-Reel deal can eat ten times the hours you priced for.

3. Usage rights (this is the big one)

Usage rights is the clause that decides how much your work is actually worth. It says where the brand can use your content, for how long, and in what contexts.

The standard creator deal is "organic usage on the brand's social channels, in perpetuity." This means the brand can repost your content on their Instagram, TikTok, etc., forever, but cannot put it in paid ads, on billboards, on their website, or in other commercial uses.

Where things get expensive is "paid usage" (or "whitelisting" or "spark ads"). This is when the brand runs your content as a paid ad, either from your handle or theirs. Paid usage typically commands an additional fee on top of the base rate, often 25 to 100 percent extra, because your face and voice are now being used in advertising at scale.

The other variable is duration. "6 months of paid usage" is very different from "perpetual paid usage." Perpetual paid usage means the brand can run your face in ads forever. That is rarely worth signing for a small flat fee, no matter how big the brand is.

Read this section carefully. If you are not sure what you are agreeing to, ask. "Can you walk me through where this content will run and for how long?" is a completely reasonable question to ask a brand manager, and any legitimate brand will answer it directly.

4. Exclusivity

Exclusivity is the clause that says you cannot work with competitors for some period of time. The variables are which competitors, for how long, and starting when.

A reasonable exclusivity for a small to mid-tier brand deal is something like "no other deals in the same product category for 30 days starting from the posting date." A common but more aggressive version is 60 or 90 days. Anything longer than 90 days for a single-post deal is unusual outside of large six-figure partnerships.

What to watch for: vague category definitions. "No competitors" is not specific. "No deals with other protein powder brands" is specific. If the contract says you can't work with "any wellness brand" for 90 days because you did a deal with a protein powder, that is overreach. Push to narrow it.

Also watch for "exclusivity periods" that start earlier than you expected. If the contract says exclusivity begins on signing rather than on posting, you might be locked out of competitor deals for the full pre-production period plus the exclusivity window. That can be months.

5. Payment terms

How much, when, and how. The most important number after the dollar amount is the payment timeline.

"Net 30" means the brand pays 30 days after they receive the invoice. "Net 60" means 60 days. "Net 90" is common with large agencies and is rough on creator cash flow. The longer the net, the longer you wait to get paid after the work is done.

Some contracts split payment: 50 percent on signing, 50 percent on posting. This is generally creator-favorable and worth pushing for, especially on first deals with a new brand. It also reduces your risk if the brand disappears halfway through the project.

Watch for clauses that tie payment to "approved performance metrics" or "verification of deliverables." That can mean the brand decides whether to pay you based on subjective judgment of how your post did. Reasonable performance-based bonuses are fine. Performance-based base payment is not, unless you specifically agreed to that model.

6. Approvals and rejections

This is the clause that says who has to approve the content before it goes live. Almost every brand requires draft approval. Some brands require approval of even small changes after draft approval.

The clause to watch for: "brand reserves the right to reject content at its sole discretion." If a brand can reject your final draft for any reason and you don't get paid, that is a significant risk. The reasonable version is something like "rejection requires written feedback and one good-faith opportunity to revise." That protects both sides.

7. Kill fees

A kill fee is what you get paid if the brand cancels the deal after you have started work. Not every contract includes one. The ones that do typically specify a percentage of the full fee.

A common structure: 25 percent kill fee if cancelled before drafts are submitted, 50 percent after draft submission, 100 percent after final approval. Without a kill fee, a brand can cancel anytime up to posting and pay you nothing for the work you have already done.

If the contract doesn't have a kill fee, ask for one. It is one of the most common reasonable asks creators make, and a legitimate brand will usually agree to at least a partial kill fee.

8. FTC and disclosure requirements

In the US, the Federal Trade Commission requires that creators disclose paid partnerships. The contract should specify how (typically "#ad" or "Paid Partnership" tag at the start of the caption). Most contracts get this right, but some try to bury the disclosure in ways the FTC doesn't actually allow, like at the end of long captions.

This one is non-negotiable on your end. The FTC enforces against creators directly, not just brands. If the contract asks you to disclose in a way that doesn't meet FTC standards, push back. It is your name on the platform, not the brand's, when an enforcement action shows up.

9. Termination clauses

What happens if either side wants out. The clause should specify how termination works for both parties, what notice is required, and what happens to work already in progress.

The version to watch for: clauses that let the brand terminate for any reason but require you to deliver everything you have produced anyway, without payment. That is one-sided and worth pushing back on.

10. Indemnification

This is the clause that says who is liable if something goes wrong. Standard creator contracts include a clause where the creator indemnifies the brand against claims arising from the creator's content (defamation, IP infringement, etc.).

Reasonable in principle. What is not reasonable is unlimited indemnification, where you could theoretically be on the hook for millions if something the brand did caused a lawsuit, but the contract makes you responsible. Push to cap indemnification at the contract value, or close to it.

The clauses that are often missing

Equally important: what is not in the contract. Things creators commonly forget to ask for:

Right to use the content in your own portfolio. If you make a Reel for a brand and the contract is silent on whether you can use it in your media kit, the safer reading is that you can't. Add a clause that says you retain the right to use the content in your portfolio, media kit, and case studies.

Credit if the brand reposts. If the brand reposts your content on their channels, the contract should say they will credit you with a tag or mention. Many do not specify this.

Force majeure that protects you too. Most contracts have a force majeure clause (acts of God, natural disasters, etc.) that protects the brand. Make sure it works both ways.

How to read a contract before you sign

You don't need to memorize legal language. You need a process. Here is one that works:

First pass: read the whole thing without trying to understand every word. Get a sense of the structure. Where are the dollar amounts? Where are the dates? Where is the usage rights section?

Second pass: for each of the clauses above, find where it lives in the contract and read it carefully. Note anything that feels off or unclear.

Third pass: write a short list of questions and ask the brand manager. Things like "can you confirm the usage rights are organic-only, not paid?" or "is there a kill fee structure?" Most issues come from misunderstanding rather than malice. Asking saves headaches later.

For tighter deals or first contracts with a brand, getting a real lawyer to look at it is genuinely worth the few hundred dollars. What to push back on (and how)

New creators often think contracts are non-negotiable. They almost always are. Brand managers expect some back and forth, and the worst case is they say no.

Reasonable asks to make on almost any deal:

50/50 payment split (half upfront, half on posting). A kill fee structure if there isn't one. Capped revisions. Narrow exclusivity by category, not by industry. A clause letting you use the content in your own portfolio. Defined usage rights with a stated duration, not "in perpetuity" by default.

The phrasing matters. "I can't sign this" sounds confrontational. "I'd like to suggest a few small adjustments before signing, can we look at clauses 4, 7, and 9?" sounds professional. Brand managers deal with contract negotiations every day. They are not surprised when a creator asks for changes. They are surprised when a creator signs without reading.

One more honest thing

The first time you read a brand contract, it will feel intimidating. The second time, it will feel less so. By the fifth or sixth contract, you will start to recognize the same clauses appearing in different language, and the whole thing will become routine.

The goal is not to become a contract expert. The goal is to know enough that when something looks off, you notice it, and you ask. That is most of the work right there.

Brand deals are real business. A contract is real paperwork. Treating it with the same care you would treat a tax return or a lease is not paranoia, it is professionalism. The creators who do this build longer careers with fewer disasters than the creators who skim and sign.
