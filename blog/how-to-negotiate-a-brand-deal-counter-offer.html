How to Negotiate a Counter-Offer Without Killing the Deal

A brand sends you an offer. You open it expecting somewhere in the range you had in mind, and instead the number is half. Maybe a third. Your first instinct is to feel insulted, type out a stiff reply, and either accept because you don't want to lose it or fire back so hard the brand quietly disappears. Most creators do one or the other. Both leave money on the table.

The real move is in between. You can counter a low offer, hold a real number, and keep the deal alive, but only if you understand what's actually being negotiated. The dollar figure is the most visible part. It is rarely the most important part.

This post is the tactical version of countering a brand offer. We will go through how to interpret what they sent, what to anchor on besides money, the specific phrasings that work, what to do when the brand says "that's our budget," and when walking away is the right call. By the end you will have a repeatable framework you can use on every offer that comes in lower than you want.

Read the offer before you react

The first mistake most creators make is reading the dollar figure and replying within an hour. Slow down. A brand offer has at least four moving parts, and the dollar figure is only one of them.

The deliverables: how many posts, what format, on which platforms, in what timeframe. One Reel is not the same as one Reel plus three Stories plus a static carousel. A "one-month campaign" is not the same as "a single piece of content."

The usage rights: what the brand can do with the content after you post it. Can they reshare it on their own channels? Can they use it in paid ads? For how long? In what regions? This is the part most creators completely ignore, and it is the part that is silently worth the most money. We will come back to this.

The exclusivity: are you blocked from working with competitors? For how long? In what category? An exclusivity clause that locks you out of an entire category for six months is a massive cost. You are giving up potential future deals to take this one.

The payment terms: Net 30? Net 60? Half upfront and half on post? Some brands quietly bury 60 or even 90 day terms in the contract. That has cash-flow implications you should price in.

Read all four before you reply to any of them. The whole package is what you're negotiating, not just the headline number.

The anchor problem

When a brand sends you a number first, they have anchored the negotiation. Everything that follows is now measured against that anchor. If they said $500 and you counter with $800, $800 feels high. If they had said $2,000 and you countered with $1,800, $1,800 would feel like a discount.

You can't always avoid being anchored low. Brand managers know this game and often open low on purpose, expecting a counter. But you can reset the anchor. The way to do it is to stop arguing about their number and put your own number on the table with context.

Instead of "$500 is too low, can you do $800?" the move is "Based on the scope you described, my rate for this type of campaign is typically $1,500. Here's what's included at that rate." Now there is a new anchor. The conversation moves from "their offer plus a small bump" to "your rate, possibly with adjustments."

This only works if you have a real rate. If you have no idea what to charge, you will fold the second they push back, and they will know. Before you counter, you need a number you actually believe in. We wrote a full piece on how to set it: how much should I charge for a brand deal. The TLDR is start from your audience value (CPM benchmarks, engagement, niche), not from what feels comfortable.

The four levers

When the cash number is fixed, you negotiate the other levers. Most creators don't know these exist, which is why they take "that's our budget" as the end of the conversation. It usually isn't.

Lever one: scope reduction. If their budget is $500 and your rate for what they asked for is $1,500, you have two choices. Negotiate up, or reduce scope. "I can do a single Reel for $500, but the carousel and Stories package would be $1,500. Want to start with the Reel and see how it performs?" This gives them a yes-able option without you discounting your rate.

Lever two: usage rights and whitelisting. The most underpriced part of every brand deal. If they want to use your content in paid ads or whitelist your account to run ads as you, that is a separate ask that should carry its own fee. A common structure: organic posting is the base rate, paid usage rights add 50 to 100 percent on top, whitelisting (where the brand runs ads through your handle) adds another 50 to 100 percent. If the brand asks for "all usage rights in perpetuity" and is offering organic-only money, that's where you push hard. Either limit the rights or raise the price.

Lever three: exclusivity. If they want category exclusivity, that has to be paid for and time-limited. "I can do 30 days of category exclusivity at no extra cost, but 90 days would be an additional $X because it means turning down other deals in this space." Real number. Real cost. Real conversation.

Lever four: payment terms. Net 60 is a hidden cost. So is "all due on final post acceptance" when the brand controls when acceptance happens. Push for 50 percent upfront when possible. If they won't budge on payment timing, you can sometimes get a higher headline number in exchange for accepting slower terms. "I can do Net 60 at $2,000, or I'd need $1,700 at Net 15." That's a real trade.

Most negotiations get stuck because both sides are only talking about lever one. Bringing levers two through four into the conversation gives you four times the surface area to find a deal that works.

The actual counter-offer email

Here's the shape of an email that actually works. Not what you would say to a friend, not what you would say to defend yourself in court. What works in a brand negotiation.

Hi [name],

Thanks for sending over the campaign details. I'd love to work on this with [brand].

The scope you described (one Reel, three Stories, full usage rights for 6 months) is in the range of $2,200 for me. The $800 offer was a good starting point but doesn't cover the paid usage piece, which typically adds 50 to 75 percent to organic rates.

Two options I'd suggest:

1. Keep the scope as described at $2,200. Full deliverables, 6 months paid usage.
2. Trim to one Reel plus organic posting rights only at $900. Same content quality, smaller package.

Happy to talk through either. Whichever lines up better with the campaign budget on your side.

Best,
[your name]

Notice the structure. You gave them context for your number, not just a higher number. You presented two paths, not one. The second path is a graceful yes-able option at a lower cash figure with reduced scope, so the brand manager has somewhere to land if their budget really is tight. And you closed with curiosity, not an ultimatum.

When the brand says "that's our budget"

This is the moment that separates creators who get good rates from creators who don't. The brand will say "that's our budget" almost every time. Sometimes it's true. Often it isn't. Your move is the same either way.

Don't argue with the budget claim. Acknowledge it and reframe.

"Totally understand if $800 is the ceiling on this campaign. At that number I'd want to scale the deliverables to match, since the original package at that rate doesn't quite work for me. Could we do just the Reel with organic rights only, instead of the full package?"

This does three things. It accepts their budget without folding on your rate. It puts the scope back on the table as a lever. And it invites them to either confirm the lower scope (good outcome) or reveal that the budget was less fixed than they said (also good outcome).

If they say no to the reduced scope and won't move on price, you have your answer. Walk away politely. We'll get to that.

The mistakes that kill deals

A few patterns to watch for. These are the ones that lose deals creators should have closed.

Apologizing for your rate. "I know this might be high but..." is a signal to the brand manager that you don't believe in your number. If you don't believe it, why should they? State your rate as a fact, not a request.

Counter-anchoring too low. If they offered $500 and your rate is $2,000, don't counter with $900 to "meet in the middle." You'll end up at $700 after the next round. Counter at $2,000 with full context. You can come down from there. You cannot easily go up.

Negotiating against yourself. "My rate is $2,000... but I could maybe do $1,500." You just dropped your own number before they even responded. Stop talking after you state your rate. Send the email. Let them respond.

Making it personal. "I have 50K followers and great engagement, I'm worth more than this." Brand managers don't care about your follower count in a vacuum. They care about whether you can deliver on the campaign. Frame everything around scope, deliverables, and rights, not about you.

Saying yes too fast on the second email. If the brand comes back with a small bump ($800 to $1,000) and you immediately accept, you signaled that you had a wider range than you initially claimed. Take 24 hours. Reply with a thoughtful counter or a confirmation, not an instant yes.

When to walk away

Not every deal should close. The walk-away decision is its own skill. Here are the situations where walking is the right call.

The brand wants full usage rights, exclusivity, and whitelisting for organic-only money, and won't budge on any of it. They are trying to buy a $5,000 package for $500. They know what they're doing. Pass.

The contract has terms you can't fix. We covered this in what to put in your brand deal contract: unlimited revisions, perpetual rights, broad indemnification clauses with no cap. If the brand won't redline these, the deal is more downside than upside.

The brand is rude, dismissive, or treating you like a vendor instead of a partner. That doesn't get better after you sign. It gets worse, because now they have leverage. The way a brand manager treats you in the negotiation is the floor of how they will treat you during the campaign.

The math doesn't work even at your acceptable rate. Sometimes a deal sounds fine on paper but the time it'll take to deliver (concepting, shooting, editing, posting, reporting) plus the opportunity cost of not pitching other brands makes it not worth the hours. Do the math. A $1,500 deal that takes 25 hours is $60 an hour. That's fine for some creators, not for others.

Walking away politely is a feature, not a failure. "Thanks for the offer. The numbers don't quite work for me on this campaign, but I'd love to be considered for future projects when the scope or budget shifts. Best of luck with the launch." Done. They will remember the professionalism. Some of them come back six months later with a real budget.

What to do once a deal closes

Once you've negotiated to a yes, the work shifts to delivery and to making the next deal easier. A few habits that compound over time.

Log every deal you close: brand, scope, rights, exclusivity, final rate, hours spent. After delivery, follow up on the invoice. Net 30 means a brand will pay 30 days after you invoice, not 30 days after you post. Send the invoice the day you post. We covered the mechanics in how to write your first invoice as a creator.

The honest summary

Brand deal negotiation is not about being the toughest person in the email thread. It's about being the most prepared. Read the whole offer, not just the dollar figure. Understand the four levers (cash, scope, rights, exclusivity, terms) and know which ones you can move on. State your rate with context, not apology. Offer two paths instead of one ultimatum. Walk away when the math doesn't work.

Most creators leave money on the table because they don't know what's being negotiated. Once you do, the same campaign that came in at $800 can close at $2,000 plus better terms, with the brand manager grateful you made it easy. That's the goal. Make it easy for them to say yes to a number that works for you.

Open the next offer that comes in. Read all four parts. Counter with context. See what happens.
