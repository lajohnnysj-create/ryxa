How to Get Brands to Notice You When You're Small

There is a myth that brand deals start at 100,000 followers. They do not. Brands work with small creators constantly, and in some ways they prefer them. A creator with 4,000 engaged followers in a specific niche can be worth more to the right brand than a creator with 90,000 scattered, half-interested ones. The problem is almost never your size. The problem is that brands cannot find you, cannot tell what you are good at, and have no easy way to say yes.

This is fixable, and none of the fixes require you to be bigger. They require you to be findable, legible, and easy to work with. Here is how small creators actually get noticed.

Why brands work with small creators at all

It helps to understand what a brand is actually buying, because it is not followers. A brand is buying trust and attention inside a specific audience. A creator with 5,000 followers who genuinely loves a niche, posts consistently, and gets real comments and saves is selling something a large generic account cannot: an audience that listens.

Small creators also cost less, carry less risk, and tend to produce more authentic content because the partnership has not become routine. A brand running a campaign would often rather work with ten small creators than one large one. That spreads risk, reaches ten different pockets of an audience, and produces ten pieces of content instead of one. You are not competing against the big accounts for that budget. You are a different line item entirely.

The shift that changes everything: stop thinking of yourself as too small to be worth a brand's time, and start thinking of yourself as a precise, low-risk way for a brand to reach a specific group of people. That is a real product. You just have to package it.

Step one: have proof before you ask for anything

The single most common reason a small creator gets ignored is that there is nothing for a brand to evaluate. A brand contact opens your profile, sees a few posts, cannot tell who your audience is or whether you can actually make good content, and moves on. They are not being cruel. They have twenty other creators to look at and no information to go on.

Before you do any outreach, you need proof. Proof is not follower count. Proof is evidence that you can do the thing a brand is paying for. The good news is you can build it yourself, today, with no brand involved.

Make content about products you already use and love, with no sponsorship, as if you had been paid. This does two things. It shows a brand exactly what a partnership with you would look like, and it shows them you can talk about a product naturally without sounding like an ad. A brand looking at three pieces of unpaid, well made content about products in their category will trust you far more than a creator who only ever posts about their own life and then asks for money. You have done the audition before being asked.

Step two: make your audience legible

Brands do not just want reach. They want to know who they are reaching. A creator who can say "my audience is mostly women in their late twenties interested in home cooking, 60 percent in the US, with strong engagement on weekday evenings" is far more hireable than a creator who can only say "I have 8,000 followers."

You almost certainly already have this information in your platform analytics. The work is collecting it, understanding it, and being able to present it cleanly. If you are not sure your numbers even look the way you think they do, it is worth doing an honest audit of your own account first. Our guide on how to spot fake Instagram followers is written for checking other accounts, but the same signals tell you whether your own engagement is as healthy as your follower count suggests. Brands check this. You should check it first.

Step three: build a media kit

A media kit is the single document that turns "some creator" into "a creator a brand can evaluate in thirty seconds." It is a short, clean summary of who you are, who your audience is, what you make, and how to work with you. It is the difference between a brand having to dig and a brand being handed everything they need.

A media kit does not need to be long or fancy. It needs your name and niche, your platforms and follower counts, your real engagement and audience demographics, a few examples of your best content, and a clear line on how to contact you. What a brand wants to see in under a minute:

  • Your niche, stated plainly, in one line
  • Where you post and how big each audience is
  • Real engagement, not just follower count
  • Who your audience actually is (age, location, interests)
  • Two or three pieces of your best work
  • One obvious way to contact you

Step four: do outreach, do not just wait

Waiting to be discovered is the slowest path that exists. The creators who land deals early are almost always the ones who reach out first. This feels uncomfortable, and it feels like you are not big enough to deserve it, but a short, specific, well aimed pitch lands more often than people expect.

The mistake is sending a long message about yourself. Brands do not need your life story. They need to know, fast, why you and their product fit together. A good pitch is three or four sentences: who you are and your niche, who your audience is, one concrete idea for content involving their product, and a link to your media kit. That is the whole message. The content idea is what separates a pitch from a beg. It shows you have actually thought about their brand, not just emailed everyone in your category.

A pitch that works has four parts:

  • One line on who you are and your niche
  • One line on who your audience is and why it matches their customer
  • One specific content idea for their product, not a generic offer
  • A link to your media kit, so they can verify everything without asking

Aim small at first. Local businesses, newer brands, and small companies in your exact niche are far more likely to say yes than a household name with an agency screening every message. Your first few deals exist to build a track record. Once you can say "I have worked with these three brands," the next pitch is easier, and the one after that is easier still.

Step five: be easy to work with

This is the part nobody talks about, and it is the reason small creators get a second and third deal. Once a brand says yes, the experience of working with you decides whether they ever come back. Reply to messages. Hit deadlines. Send the content when you said you would. Keep the contract, the deliverables, and the payment details in one place so nothing slips.

Brands talk to each other, and they keep lists of creators who were easy. A small creator who is professional and reliable will out-earn a larger creator who is chaotic, because the brand does not have to chase them. As your deals grow, tracking them stops being optional. The first brand deal is the hardest. Not because of your size, but because you have no proof and no track record yet. Everything above is about manufacturing that proof before anyone asks for it, so the first yes comes sooner.

The honest timeline

None of this is instant. You will pitch brands that never reply. You will get a few polite nos. That is normal, and it is not a verdict on you. Outreach is a numbers game with a quality filter on top: specific, well aimed pitches sent consistently will land deals, and scattered generic ones will not.

What matters is that you stop treating brand deals as something that happens to you once you are big enough, and start treating them as something you build toward on purpose. The small creator who has proof, a clean media kit, and the nerve to send a good pitch will be working with brands long before the creator who is just waiting to be found.
