Hobby vs Business: When Does the IRS Treat Your Creator Income as a Real Business?

Educational content only. This article explains general concepts that US content creators encounter around the IRS hobby vs business classification. It is not tax, legal, or financial advice. Tax rules vary by year and individual situation. Talk to a qualified CPA or tax professional for advice specific to your circumstances. The information here is current as of 2026 but rules change.

Every content creator who starts making money eventually runs into the same question. You made $1,200 from a brand deal, $400 from affiliate links, and $80 from a tiny digital download. Tax season is coming. Is this a business? Is it a hobby? Does the IRS even care? And if you call it a hobby, can you still write off your camera and your editing software?

The short version: the IRS does care, the rules are not arbitrary, and getting the classification wrong in either direction costs creators money. Calling business income "hobby income" means you cannot deduct expenses, which can push your tax bill thousands of dollars higher than it needs to be. Calling hobby income "business income" without acting like a business can invite an audit and trigger penalties. This post explains how the IRS actually decides which side of the line your creator work falls on, and what creators commonly get wrong.

Why the Hobby vs Business Distinction Matters

Before the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, hobby income and business income were taxed differently but creators could still deduct some hobby expenses as miscellaneous itemized deductions. Those deductions were largely eliminated. As of 2026, the rules look like this:

  • If the IRS treats your creator work as a business, you report income and expenses on Schedule C of your 1040. You can deduct legitimate business expenses (camera, software, home office, internet, mileage, contractor payments) against your income. You owe self-employment tax on net profit, but the tax base is income minus expenses.
  • If the IRS treats your creator work as a hobby, you report the income as "other income" on Schedule 1 of your 1040. You pay regular income tax on the full amount. You cannot deduct expenses against it. A creator who earned $5,000 and spent $3,000 on production pays tax on the full $5,000, not the $2,000 of net profit.

This is why the distinction matters. A creator with $20,000 in gross income and $10,000 in legitimate expenses pays tax on $10,000 if classified as a business, and on $20,000 if classified as a hobby. The difference can easily be $2,500 to $3,500 in federal tax alone, before state tax stacks on. Multiply that across years and the cost of misclassification adds up fast.

Hobby classification means you owe tax on gross income without deducting a single dollar of expenses. For most creators, that is the worst possible outcome.

The 9-Factor Test the IRS Actually Uses

The IRS does not use a simple income threshold to decide hobby vs business. There is no magic dollar amount where creator income flips from hobby to business. Instead, the IRS evaluates a set of factors (laid out in Treasury Regulation 1.183-2) that look at how you treat the activity. No single factor is decisive. The IRS weighs them together.

The 9 factors the IRS considers:

  • Do you operate in a businesslike manner? Separate bank account, bookkeeping, invoices, contracts, a brand deal CRM or accounting software. The more your operation looks like a small business, the stronger the case.
  • Do you have expertise in the activity? Have you studied content strategy, taken courses, consulted with experts, or learned from creators in your niche? Self-taught counts.
  • How much time and effort do you put in? A creator who works 25 hours a week on content looks more like a business than one who posts whenever they feel like it.
  • Do you expect the assets to appreciate? Less relevant for most creators (you are not buying real estate), but a growing audience and brand can count as an appreciating asset.
  • Have you been successful in similar activities? Past entrepreneurial or freelance experience supports a profit motive.
  • What is your history of income or losses? Continual losses with no path to profit suggest hobby. Losses in early years followed by climbing revenue suggests a business in its startup phase.
  • Have you ever turned a profit? The IRS pays attention to the famous "3 of 5 years" guideline (more on this below), but profitability in any year strengthens the business argument.
  • Is the activity your livelihood or supplemental? Full-time creators have a stronger case than nights-and-weekends creators, but supplemental income can still be a business.
  • Is there personal pleasure or recreation involved? Most creator activities have a "fun" component, which can weaken the business case. The counterweight is whether you do the work systematically regardless of whether it feels fun.

The reason there is no bright line is that creator work genuinely sits in the gray zone the IRS designed these factors for. Most creators enjoy the work. Most have variable income. Most started as hobbyists who slowly professionalized. The 9-factor test is the IRS asking "did you mean to run a business, and are you behaving like one?"

The "3 of 5 Years" Profit Rule

This is the most-cited rule and the most-misunderstood. The actual rule (IRC Section 183(d)) is that if your activity is profitable in 3 of the last 5 years (including the current year), there is a legal presumption that it is engaged in for profit, which makes it a business. The activity passes this safe harbor and the IRS has the burden to prove otherwise.

The misunderstanding is the inverse: many creators believe that if they do NOT have 3 profitable years out of 5, the IRS automatically calls it a hobby. That is not the case. The presumption only flips in your favor when you hit 3 of 5. When you do not, the IRS still uses the 9-factor test. Many legitimate businesses lose money for the first 3 to 5 years and remain businesses. The 3-of-5 rule is a shortcut, not a death sentence.

The practical implications for creators:

  • If you have been profitable in 3 of the last 5 years, you are in the clear. The safe harbor applies and you should be reporting on Schedule C.
  • If you have not yet hit 3 profitable years, you can still be a business. You just lose the automatic presumption, and the IRS evaluates you on the 9 factors above.
  • Continual losses with no business plan or improvement signal hobby. If your creator activity has lost money for 5 years and there is no plausible reason to expect profit, the IRS becomes much more skeptical.

"Profit" for these rules means net profit, not gross revenue. A creator who earned $8,000 in gross brand deal income and had $9,500 in expenses had a net loss of $1,500 for that year, which counts as an unprofitable year regardless of how much money technically came in.

What Counts as Creator "Business" Activity

Creator income that the IRS readily treats as business income when the 9-factor test points toward business:

  • Brand deals and sponsored content (Instagram posts, TikTok videos, YouTube integrations, podcast ads)
  • Affiliate commissions from links you place in content
  • Ad revenue from platforms (YouTube AdSense, TikTok Creator Fund, Facebook monetization)
  • Digital product sales (presets, templates, ebooks, courses, memberships)
  • Coaching and consulting fees (1:1 sessions, group coaching, paid Q&A)
  • UGC and freelance content created for brands without posting on your own accounts
  • Subscription revenue (Patreon, Substack paid tiers, YouTube channel memberships)
  • Speaking fees, licensing, and merchandise sales

All of these can be hobby income too, depending on context. A teenager who occasionally gets free product in exchange for an Instagram post is probably a hobbyist. A creator who consistently brings in revenue from any of the above, treats it like work, tracks every dollar, and is trying to grow that revenue is almost certainly a business.

How to Position Your Creator Work as a Business

If your goal is to be classified as a business (and for most creators making real money, it should be), the work is mostly behavioral. The IRS looks at whether you operate like a business, not whether you have filed certain paperwork. Things that strengthen the business case:

  • Keep a separate bank account for creator income and expenses. Even if you are a sole proprietor with no LLC, a dedicated account makes the business-vs-personal line clearer.
  • Track every brand deal, payment, and expense systematically. This is the single biggest signal. A creator who can hand a CPA a clean ledger looks like a business. A creator who reconstructs the year from screenshots and bank statements looks like a hobbyist.
  • Sign written contracts with brands. Even a simple one-page agreement looks more businesslike than a DM thread.
  • Send invoices for brand deals. A PDF invoice with payment terms is a tiny effort and a strong signal.
  • Have a website, media kit, and professional contact email. See our guide on what a media kit actually is and when you need one. The media kit alone tells the IRS this is a marketed business.
  • Set aside time and act on a content schedule. Posting regularly with a strategy looks more business-like than posting whenever inspiration strikes.
  • Reinvest in the business. New equipment, software subscriptions, courses, contractors, ads. Reinvestment is one of the clearest signals of profit motive.
  • Track your income against your expenses. The point is to be able to show that you are aiming for profit and adjusting based on what works.

Most of these are things creators are already doing or should be doing anyway. The IRS classification is mostly a downstream effect of running your creator work professionally. The same workflow that helps you make better decisions about pricing, brand deals, and content strategy also makes the business classification easy to defend.

Common Creator Misconceptions

"I made under $600 so I do not have to report it."

The $600 threshold is when a brand is required to send you a 1099-NEC. It is not the threshold at which income becomes taxable. All income is reportable, regardless of whether a 1099 was issued. The IRS expects you to report every dollar of creator income whether or not anyone sent you paperwork. This is covered in more detail in our post on how to track brand deal income for taxes.

"It is only a hobby until I quit my day job."

The IRS does not require you to be full-time for an activity to be a business. Plenty of legitimate businesses are run on nights and weekends. What matters is whether you are operating with profit motive and businesslike behavior, not how many hours per week you put in.

"If I do not claim deductions, the IRS will not bother me."

Choosing to skip deductions does not actually protect you from misclassification. If the IRS audits and reclassifies your activity from hobby to business or vice versa, you owe the difference in tax either way. The right move is to classify correctly the first time based on how you actually operate.

"I have to form an LLC for it to be a business."

This is the most common misconception. An LLC is a legal entity that mostly affects liability protection and, in some cases, how you pay taxes. It does not control hobby vs business classification. You can be a sole proprietor with no LLC and run a legitimate business. You can also form an LLC and still be treated as a hobby if you do not operate with profit motive. The Gray Zone (and When to Get Help)

Most creators are not on the extreme ends of this spectrum. The clear cases are easy: a college student who posts memes for fun and made $40 from a single affiliate link is a hobbyist. A creator pulling $80,000 a year from brand deals, courses, and coaching with separate bank accounts and a CPA is a business. The gray zone is everyone in between.

If you are in the gray zone, the practical signal is whether you are trying to grow this into something real. If you are pricing brand deals deliberately (we wrote about how to set creator brand deal pricing), tracking every dollar, reinvesting in your work, and treating it as a real revenue stream, you are running a business in the eyes of the IRS even if your income is modest. If you are doing this casually with no intention of growing or systematizing, you are probably a hobbyist.

Once your creator income reaches a few thousand dollars a year and you are taking deductions, it is worth a 30-minute call with a CPA who has worked with creators or freelancers. The cost of one consultation is typically less than the tax you would save by being correctly classified and claiming legitimate deductions you might otherwise miss.

The Practical Bottom Line

The IRS does not care whether you call yourself a creator, an influencer, a freelancer, or a self-employed digital media producer. It cares whether you operate with profit motive and behave like a business. If you do, your creator work is a business, and you should be reporting it on Schedule C with full deductions. If you do not, it is a hobby, and the tax cost is significantly higher.
