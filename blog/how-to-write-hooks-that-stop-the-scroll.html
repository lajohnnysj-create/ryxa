How to Write Hooks That Stop the Scroll

The first three seconds of a Reel, TikTok, or YouTube Short do more work than the next 27 combined. If you don't stop the scroll in that window, the algorithm sees a low retention number and the video is buried. The rest of the video could be the best thing you ever made. Doesn't matter. Nobody saw it.

This is the part of short-form content most creators underinvest in. They spend two hours on the edit and 30 seconds on the hook. The math is backwards. The hook is where the views are won or lost. The body is where the views are kept.

This post is the tactical breakdown. We will cover what a hook actually has to do (which is not what most people think), four hook patterns that consistently work, the audio-first rule that separates good short-form from bad short-form, and the structural mistakes that kill retention before the algorithm ever has a chance to push the video. By the end you'll have a framework you can apply to the next video you film.

What a hook actually has to do

A hook does not have to be loud. It does not have to be a yelled question with a quick zoom. Those work for some creators in some niches, but they are a style choice, not the underlying mechanic. The mechanic is simpler.

A hook has to do exactly one thing: make the viewer's brain ask a question that requires watching the rest of the video to answer. That's it. If the brain asks "wait, what?" or "how does that work?" or "what happens next?" in the first second, the viewer stays. If the brain asks nothing, the thumb moves.

This reframes the whole problem. You are not trying to be clever or shocking or weird. You are trying to create a tiny open loop in the viewer's brain. Open loops want to close. The viewer keeps watching to close them.

Every good hook is a setup that implies there is a payoff coming. The bad hooks tell you the payoff in the first three seconds, which gives the viewer no reason to stay. "Five tips for better lighting" promises a list. The viewer can imagine the list. They scroll. "I shot this entire video with a $20 lamp from Target" promises a specific outcome. The viewer wants to see the lamp. They stay.

The four hook patterns that work

Most working hooks fall into one of four patterns. None of these are new. They've been used in storytelling since before video existed. What's new is how fast they have to land.

Pattern one: the surprising claim. A statement that sounds wrong, contradicts conventional wisdom, or makes a specific bold prediction. "Most engagement coaching is advice from people who haven't grown an account in three years." "You can get more reach on a Tuesday than on a Saturday." "Your first 1,000 followers are worth more than your next 10,000." The brain hears it and immediately wants to know if it's true. That's the loop.

Pattern two: the specific outcome. A precise, often surprising result that the video will explain how to reach. Specificity matters. "How I made $4,200 from a single Instagram carousel" works. "How to make money on Instagram" doesn't. Numbers, time frames, and specific objects create curiosity that vague promises don't.

Pattern three: the question the viewer was already asking. Name the thing they were already wondering about. "Why do your friends never engage with your posts?" "How come some of your Reels get 2K views and others get 200K from the same account?" If the question maps to something the viewer has been quietly puzzled about, they will pause to find out the answer.

Pattern four: the visual cold open. Skip the words. Start mid-action, mid-result, or mid-something-that-shouldn't-be-happening. A close-up of something burning. A blank page filling with text. A perfectly arranged scene about to be destroyed. Visual mystery works the same way as verbal mystery: the brain pauses to figure out what it's looking at, and by the time it does, the viewer is committed.

The mistake creators make is mixing patterns or stacking them. A hook that is simultaneously a surprising claim AND a question AND a visual mystery comes across as trying too hard. Pick one. Execute it cleanly. The hook does its job in two to three seconds and hands off to the body.

The audio-first rule

This is the part most creators miss. Short-form video is consumed with sound on, on a phone, often by a viewer doing something else with their eyes (cooking, walking, half-watching while scrolling). The hook is heard before it's seen.

That means the first words out of your mouth, or the first audio cue, is doing 70 percent of the hook work. The visual reinforces. The audio commits.

Test this on your own scrolling. Most of the videos that stop you, the voice or sound caught your attention before the image did. A specific phrase, a question, a sound effect, a music cue. Then your eyes registered the image.

What this means in practice: write the hook as a line of dialogue or a sound design choice, not as a visual concept. "An empty fridge slowly fills with food while a voice says 'I tracked everything I ate for 30 days and learned something I wasn't expecting'" is a hook. "A montage of food shots" is not. The first one tells the viewer what to listen for. The second one assumes the viewer is watching closely. They aren't.

Captions help, especially for muted viewing in feeds. But captions are a fallback, not a primary channel. Write for audio, caption for accessibility, design the visual to match. In that order.

The structural mistakes

Even with a strong hook concept, a few structural issues will tank retention before the body of the video has a chance. These are the patterns to watch for in the edit.

The intro that delays the hook. "Hey guys, welcome back to my channel, today we're going to talk about..." is the death sentence. The viewer is gone before you finish "welcome back." If your video has any kind of intro beat, cut it. The hook is the first frame.

The repeat hook. Saying the same thing twice ("Most engagement coaching is bad. Most engagement coaching is genuinely terrible advice from...") feels emphatic in your head and reads as padding in the feed. Say it once. Move.

The slow visual reveal. If your hook is a visual mystery, the reveal needs to happen before viewers lose patience. Roughly two to three seconds is the tolerance. A 7-second mystery is the same as no hook.

The vague promise. "Today I'm going to share my best tip for growing on Instagram" is not a hook. It's a label. There is no specific outcome implied, no surprising claim, no visual mystery. The brain has nothing to attach to. Replace vague promises with specific claims.

The disconnected payoff. If the hook promises one thing and the body delivers something else, the viewer feels tricked. Even if the body is genuinely good, the trust break shows up in completion rate, comments, and downstream views. Match the body to the hook precisely.

Writing the hook before you film

The single biggest workflow upgrade most creators can make is writing the hook before they pick up the camera, not after. Most short-form videos get filmed first, then the creator tries to retrofit a hook to whatever footage they ended up with. That works occasionally. Usually it produces a soft opening because the visual material wasn't shot with the hook in mind.

The better order is: pick a hook pattern, write the first line, design the first visual to support it, then film. If you're filming a tutorial, the first frame should be the result, not the setup. If you're filming a story, the first frame should be the moment of tension, not the context.

Testing and iterating

Hooks are testable. The metric is straightforward: 3-second retention. Almost every short-form platform reports it. If your video is at 90 percent retention at the 3-second mark, your hook is doing its job. If you're at 40 percent, the hook is broken. The body could still be great. The body just isn't reaching anyone.

Treat hooks like a separate iteration loop from the rest of the video. Same body, different first line. Same body, different first visual. Same body, written as a question vs. as a surprising claim. After three or four hook variations of the same underlying content, you'll start to see which patterns consistently get the higher hold rate for your audience and niche.

This is the same principle that drives the thumbnail iteration loop on YouTube. We wrote a full piece on the thumbnail side in how to make YouTube thumbnails that get clicks. The mechanics are different (thumbnail = visual + text decision, short-form hook = audio + visual + first-line decision) but the iteration habit is identical: change one variable at a time, measure what changes, keep what works.

What this looks like across platforms

Hook mechanics translate across Reels, TikTok, and Shorts, with small differences. Worth knowing them.

On Instagram Reels, the audio bias is strongest. Trending audio with a strong hook moment baked into the first second can do a lot of the lift, but the visual cue has to match. Static talking-head Reels need an exceptionally strong first line because there's no other channel doing work.

On TikTok, the cold open is more accepted and even expected. TikTok viewers are conditioned to a confusing first frame that pays off later. You can be weirder, more visual-first, more unconventional in the opening than on Reels.

On YouTube Shorts, the viewer demographic skews slightly older and the platform overlaps with longer-form content. Hooks that imply a real informational payoff tend to outperform hooks that rely on visual mystery alone. Closer to the Reels model than the TikTok one.

The honest summary

Hooks are not magic. They are a craft. The brain wants open loops, and a hook is the smallest possible open loop you can create in the first three seconds of a video. Pick one of four patterns: surprising claim, specific outcome, named question, visual mystery. Write for audio first, visual second. Cut anything before the hook. Match the body to the hook so the payoff feels earned.

If you film 20 videos in the next month with this framework applied to every single hook, two things will happen. Your 3-second retention will climb meaningfully, and the videos that don't will tell you something specific about what doesn't land for your audience. That's information you can use. Hook craft compounds. Most creators never invest in it, so the ones who do open up a measurable gap.
