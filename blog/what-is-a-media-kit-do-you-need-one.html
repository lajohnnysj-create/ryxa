Media Kit

What Is a Media Kit and Do You Actually Need One?

If you've ever talked to another creator who's been doing brand deals for a while, somewhere in the conversation they probably said "send me your media kit" like it was a thing everyone has. And then you went home and Googled "what is a media kit" and got 47 different answers, half of them trying to sell you a Canva template.

Here's the actual answer. What a media kit is, what's actually in one, when you genuinely need one, when you don't, and what most of the templates online get wrong.

What a Media Kit Actually Is

A media kit is a single document (or web page) that gives a brand everything they need to decide whether to work with you, in one place, without having to ask you for it.

That's the whole job. It collapses the back-and-forth of "what platforms are you on, what's your audience size, who's your audience, what have you done before, what are your rates" into one link or one PDF the brand can review and forward internally.

The good ones answer the questions brands have. The bad ones look pretty but don't help anyone make a decision. Most creator media kits online are bad ones.

Do You Actually Need One?

The honest answer: it depends on where you are.

You probably don't need one yet if...

  • You've done fewer than 3 paid brand deals total
  • You're under 5,000 followers on your main platform
  • You haven't been approached by a brand in writing yet
  • You're mostly thinking about this in the abstract, not because someone asked

If that's you, focus on growing the audience and getting one or two deals done. The kit will write itself once you have data to put in it. Building a media kit before you have anything to put in it is a form of productive procrastination.

You probably do need one if...

  • Brands are reaching out and asking "do you have a media kit?"
  • You've done a handful of deals and the negotiations keep stalling because brands don't know basic facts about your audience
  • You're actively pitching brands (cold outreach) and need something to attach
  • You're at 10K+ followers on your main platform and starting to take brand work seriously
  • You're working with an agency or manager and they keep asking for one

If you're in any of those buckets, a media kit isn't optional. The deals you lose without one aren't deals you'll know about, which is part of what makes it easy to put off.

A media kit doesn't get you brand deals. It removes friction from the deals you'd otherwise lose to friction.

What Brands Actually Want From a Media Kit

Before getting into what to include, it helps to know what the person on the other side is looking for. A brand's marketing manager opens your media kit with three questions:

  1. Is this creator a fit for our audience and product?
  2. What's the audience size, quality, and demographic mix?
  3. What does this person charge and what does that include?

Everything in a good media kit serves answering one of those three questions fast. If a section doesn't help answer them, it's decoration. Decoration is fine in moderation, but it shouldn't bury the answers.

What to Include (The Sections That Actually Matter)

Here's the structure that works for 95 percent of creators. Don't reinvent the wheel.

1. A short personal intro (2 to 4 sentences)

Who you are, what kind of content you make, and what your audience cares about. Keep it specific. "I'm a Brooklyn-based home cook who films 60-second weeknight recipes for working parents who don't have time to think about dinner" is worth ten times more than "I'm a lifestyle creator who loves food and inspiring my community."

The specificity is what helps a brand decide whether you're a fit. Generic intros make brands skip you because they can't tell whether you match.

2. Headshot and brand aesthetic

One clear photo of you (the kind the brand might use in a pitch deck to their boss). Plus 4 to 6 sample images of your actual content. This gives the brand a feel for the visual world your content lives in. If a brand's product is high-end minimalist and your content is maximalist neon, the visual match (or mismatch) shows up here faster than any other section.

3. Audience metrics

This is the section brands actually read carefully. Include for each platform you're active on:

  • Follower count (current, not aspirational)
  • Average engagement rate over the last 30 to 90 days (likes plus comments divided by followers, averaged across your last 10 posts)
  • Average post reach or views over the same window
  • For YouTube specifically: average views per video (this matters more than subscriber count)
  • For newsletter specifically: active subscribers and average open rate

Pull these from each platform's native analytics. Update them every 30 to 60 days. Stale numbers from 8 months ago are a tell that you don't update the kit, which makes everything else in it less credible.

4. Audience demographics

The single most underrated section, and the one most templates leave out. Include:

  • Top 3 to 5 countries with percentages (US 65%, Canada 12%, UK 8%, etc.)
  • Gender split (e.g., 72% female, 26% male, 2% non-binary/other)
  • Top age brackets with percentages (e.g., 25-34: 48%, 18-24: 23%, 35-44: 19%)
  • Top cities if relevant to your niche (e.g., for local-leaning content)

This data lives in your Instagram Insights, TikTok Studio, YouTube Studio, or LinkedIn analytics. Brands often need it to justify your deal internally to their boss or to the brand's media-buying team. A creator who has this data ready in their kit gets approved faster than one who doesn't.

5. Past brand work

Three to six examples of brand collaborations you've done. For each one, include the brand name, what you produced (a Reel, a YouTube integration, a Story series), and ideally one performance metric (views, engagements, link clicks, sales if you know them). A small logo grid of brands you've worked with helps if you have a handful.

If you haven't done many brand deals yet, organic content that performed exceptionally well also counts here. "A Reel about minimalist apartment hacks that got 2.4M views and drove 45K profile visits" is real proof of audience response, even if no money changed hands.

6. Services and rate ranges

List what you offer (Reel, in-feed post, Story series, YouTube integration, dedicated YouTube video, newsletter sponsorship, etc.) and a starting rate or range for each. You don't have to publish fixed prices. Many top-tier creators specifically don't. But you do need to anchor the conversation somewhere.

If you're not sure what to charge in the first place, that's a separate problem worth solving before you publish a kit. We covered the actual benchmarks and how to price in how much to charge for a brand deal.

7. Contact information

One email. One link. That's it. Don't make a brand hunt for how to reach you. If you have a manager or agency, list them. If you don't, list yourself.

What to Leave Out

Most creator media kits are too long. The templates online are full of sections that look professional but waste the brand's time. Cut these:

  • Long brand mission statements. "My mission is to inspire and empower the next generation of..." Brands skim past this every time. The intro section does this job in 3 sentences.
  • A page of testimonials from non-brand people. Your audience saying nice things about you is sweet. It's not what a brand needs to make a decision.
  • Generic "what I value as a creator" content. Authenticity, community, creativity. Everyone says these. They don't differentiate.
  • Page after page of content screenshots. 4 to 6 thumbnails is enough. More is filler.
  • Animated transitions, parallax scrolling, video backgrounds. If it slows the kit down or makes it harder to skim, cut it. The brand is reviewing 15 kits today. Friction loses.
  • Old engagement screenshots. One old screenshot of a post that did 500K views, undated, with no context, isn't proof. It's filler that makes the rest of your data look suspect.

The Format: PDF, Web Page, or Both?

Three options, each with tradeoffs.

PDF

The traditional format. Easy for brand managers to forward internally, attach to emails, and review offline. Downsides: it goes stale immediately. The version a brand has from 6 months ago has stale numbers, and you have no idea if they're using it. Updating means re-sending.

Web page

A live URL (e.g. ryxa.io/mediakit/yourusername or a hosted media kit page) that updates whenever you update it. Brand always sees the current version. Easier to make visually rich. Lets you track if anyone's viewing it (with analytics). Downsides: some brand managers still prefer a PDF for internal forwarding, and some procurement systems can't open URLs directly.

Both, ideally

The best setup is a live media kit page that brands can view immediately, with a "download PDF" option if they need it for internal forwarding. A hosted media kit page handles this and gives you analytics on who's looking at it. Most creators who are serious about brand work now lead with the link and offer the PDF on request, not the other way around.

One specific small win: putting your media kit URL in your link-in-bio (not just a contact email) means brand managers can find it without DMing you first. Many warm leads die because the brand person didn't want to send a DM and didn't see another way to reach you.

How Often to Update It

Audience metrics every 30 to 60 days. Brand work and rates every quarter. Demographics every 90 days (they shift slowly but they do shift, especially if your content evolves). The intro and aesthetic section, only when your content meaningfully changes.

If you can't keep it updated, a live web-based kit is even more important than a PDF. At least a web kit just shows whatever you've last published. A PDF in the wild for 8 months actively misrepresents you.

The Mistakes Most Creators Make

A few specific patterns that come up over and over.

Making it look "professional" instead of useful

Heavy fonts. Stock imagery. Corporate language. The instinct is to look polished, but brands aren't hiring you because you look corporate. They're hiring you because your content is yours. The kit should look like you, not like a generic agency.

Inflating numbers

Rounding 23K followers up to "25K+" is fine. Quoting a viral post's reach as your "average" reach is a lie that gets caught the moment a brand checks your last 5 posts. Brands check. Get caught once and you're done with that brand and probably some of their network.

Padding past brand work

Listing a gifted PR box from 2 years ago as a "brand collaboration with Sephora" is technically true and obviously oversold. Brand managers can read between the lines, and inflated past work damages your credibility on the parts that are real.

Forgetting to mention what you don't do

If you don't do exclusivity deals over 30 days, or don't accept gifted-only collaborations, or don't promote certain product categories (alcohol, gambling, MLM, etc.), a one-line note saves everyone time. "I don't promote crypto or gambling-related products" is a useful filter, not a negative.

Sending it before it's asked for

If a brand sends a one-line DM ("hey, would you be interested in working with us?"), don't immediately reply with a media kit attached. Reply with a short, warm response and a question or two ("would love to hear more, what are you thinking in terms of scope and timing?"). Send the kit on the second exchange, once you know it's a real conversation. Leading with the kit can feel transactional in a way that kills warmth.

Templates vs. Building Your Own

You can buy a Canva template for $15. You can find free ones online. You can hire someone on Fiverr to design one for $80. You can build your own.

The single most important factor in whether a media kit works isn't the template. It's whether the data inside it is accurate, current, and specific to your audience. A beautiful template with stale numbers performs worse than a plain template with current ones. The format is much less important than the contents.

If a template helps you start, use one. If templates feel like a trap that's keeping you from updating it, abandon the template and just use a simple structure (even a Google Doc shared as PDF can work).

Where the Media Kit Fits in the Brand Deal Funnel

It helps to think about where a media kit actually does its work. Brand reaches out (or you pitch). You exchange a couple of DMs to scope interest. The brand wants to take the conversation seriously. That's the moment the media kit earns its keep. It moves the conversation from "let me get back to you" to "let me forward this internally" faster than answering questions one at a time would.

Once you're in active negotiation, the media kit's job is mostly done. From there, it's about the specific deliverables, rates, and contract terms. A great media kit doesn't replace that part of the process. It just makes sure you get to that part of the process in the first place.

If you want to turn your audience into real income, brand deals are usually one of the highest-leverage paths, and the media kit is the artifact that unlocks them at scale.

The Realistic Bottom Line

A media kit is a tool, not a trophy. Its only job is to give a brand the information they need to say yes to working with you, in one place, fast. The most beautiful kit in the world doesn't help if the numbers are stale, the audience description is vague, or the rates section is missing.

Build the simplest version that answers a brand's three questions (audience fit, audience size and quality, rates). Keep it updated. Lead with the link, not the PDF. And remember that the kit's job is to get you to the conversation, not to close the deal.

Once you have one and you keep it current, you'll notice something subtle: the conversations move faster. Deals don't stall as often. Brands come back. That's the kit doing its job quietly in the background, which is exactly what it's supposed to do.
