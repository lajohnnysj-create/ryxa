Creator Economy

How Platform Fees Quietly Eat Creator Income

Ryxa Team • May 2026 • 7 min read

"Just 5%."

It sounds small. It sounds reasonable. It's the number a creator-economy platform tells you when you sign up to sell your first course, your first coaching session, your first digital download. Five percent of each sale, on top of the payment processor's cut. A modest tip jar in exchange for the platform existing.

And then a year goes by, and you do the math, and the number is much bigger than five percent of one sale.

The Real Cost of "Just 5%"

Here's what happens when you actually run the numbers. Take a creator selling a $50 digital product. Average month: 40 sales. That's $2,000 in gross revenue per month.

The "small" platform fee

$2,000 monthly revenue

5% platform fee = $100/month

$1,200/year gone to platform fees

That's not counting payment processor fees (typically 2.9% + 30¢, which any platform passes through), refunds, or chargebacks.

Twelve hundred dollars a year. For one creator. On one product.

A creator selling a $200 course at the same volume? $4,800/year. A coaching service at $300 per session, eight sessions a month? $1,440/year. And the percentage is often higher than 5%. Some platforms charge 8%, 10%, even 30% on certain sale types.

The fees aren't small. They're just spread across enough small transactions that you stop noticing.

Why Creators Don't Notice

Three reasons percentage fees feel invisible even as they add up.

The fee is taken before the money hits your account.

If you charged $50 for a product and the platform takes its cut on the way through, you see $46 land in your bank account. Not $50 minus $4. Just $46. Your brain anchors to the deposit, not to the missing $4. Multiply across hundreds of transactions and you never see the cumulative number.

The fee scales with success.

Percentage-based fees feel painless when you're earning $200 a month and brutal when you're earning $20,000 a month. The platforms that take 5-10% of revenue are taking 5-10% of every dollar you ever earn through them, forever. As you scale, their take grows linearly while their cost to serve you barely changes.

This is what makes percentage-based fees structurally bad for creators. You pay the most when you can least afford to switch, because by that point you've built audience and habits around the platform.

You're comparing the platform fee to the alternative of building everything yourself.

"Yes, they take 5%, but they also handle hosting, payments, the storefront, the email receipts." This is the framing the platforms want you to use, because it's true and also because it obscures the fact that there are platforms that handle all the same things and charge a flat rate instead of a percentage of revenue.

The Compounding Effect

The math gets uglier when you stretch it across a creator career instead of looking at one month.

A 5-year projection at 5% platform fees

Year 1: $25,000 in product sales

Year 2: $50,000

Year 3: $80,000

Year 4: $120,000

Year 5: $160,000

Total revenue: $435,000

5% platform fee = $21,750 lost

Twenty-one thousand dollars. That's a vacation, an emergency fund, a quarter of a year's living expenses, the down payment on better recording equipment, three months of rent in most cities. It's enough to fund a sabbatical, hire help, or take a calculated risk on a new project.

And that's at 5%. At 10%, it's $43,500. At 15% (which some niches see), it's $65,250.

Why It Doesn't Feel Worth Switching

Here's the trap. By the time the cumulative number is big enough to matter, the cost of switching feels bigger.

You've trained your audience to find you on a specific platform. Your past customers are in their database. Your existing courses, products, or services are formatted for their specific tools. Your bookmarks, integrations, and workflows are wired to their interface. Switching means rebuilding all of that, plus hoping your audience follows.

So you stay. Not because the platform is the best fit. Because leaving is harder than absorbing the percentage. The platforms that take a percentage of every sale know this, and price accordingly. Once you're past 18 months on a platform, the friction of leaving usually exceeds another year of fees, and they have you indefinitely.

The move to make is to evaluate the math before you're locked in, not after.

The Other Way: Flat-Rate Pricing

Some platforms charge a flat monthly subscription instead of taking a percentage of sales. The math is dramatically different.

A $20/month flat subscription costs you $240 per year, regardless of whether you make $1,000 or $1 million. The percentage you pay shrinks as your revenue grows. At $25,000 annual sales, that's a 1% effective fee. At $160,000 annual sales, it's 0.15%. Compare to a flat 5% no matter what you earn.

Flat-rate pricing aligns the platform's incentive with yours. They make money when you sign up. After that, they need to keep you happy enough to stay subscribed. Percentage-based pricing aligns the platform's incentive with itself. They make more when you make more, regardless of whether the platform is actually adding more value to your business.

The platform that grows its take as you grow your business is not your partner. It's a tax.

Note on payment processor fees: Stripe, PayPal, and similar processors charge ~2.9% + 30¢ per transaction. These fees are unavoidable and any platform you use will pass them through. The question this post focuses on is whether your platform charges this AND adds their own percentage on top.

Three Questions to Ask Before You Sign Up

Before you commit to any creator monetization tool, get clear answers to these three questions. The marketing copy will not answer them. You may have to read terms of service or email support.

  • What percentage of each sale do you take, separate from payment processor fees? If the answer is anything other than zero, calculate what that costs you over a year of projected revenue.
  • Is there a flat-rate alternative? Some platforms offer both options. The flat-rate plan almost always wins past a few thousand dollars in monthly sales, but you have to know to ask.
  • What does the percentage cost me at 1x, 5x, and 10x my current revenue? Project forward. The fee that costs $40 a month today will cost $400 a month when you grow. Look at that number now.

The Practical Move

If you're already on a platform that takes a percentage of every sale, you don't have to switch tomorrow. But you should run the numbers honestly. How much did you pay in platform fees last year? Look at your statements, not the headline percentage. Add up the actual dollars.

Then ask whether what the platform delivered for those dollars was worth it. Did your storefront grow because of platform-driven traffic? Did their tools meaningfully move the needle? Or could you have run an equivalent business on a flat-rate platform and kept the percentage as profit?

For some creators, percentage-based platforms make sense. They handle complexity you couldn't otherwise manage, drive meaningful first-touch traffic, or offer features that genuinely justify their cut. Pay them.

For most creators, the honest answer is simpler. The platform takes a percentage because you let them. Once you see the cumulative number, that becomes a much harder choice to keep making.

The Bottom Line

"Just 5%" is a frame designed to make a recurring tax feel like a small one-time cost. The real cost is the multiplier across every sale you'll ever make on that platform, indefinitely.

Before you commit to any creator monetization tool, do the five-year math. Project your revenue, multiply by the percentage, and look at the number. If it's a number that would matter to you in any other context, it should matter here too.

Your audience. Your offers. Your work. The percentage that comes out of every sale belongs in your bank account, not someone else's.
