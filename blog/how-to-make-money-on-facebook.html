How to Make Money on Facebook in 2026

Ryxa 📅 April 2026 ⏱ 10 min read

Facebook gets dismissed a lot. "Isn't that for old people?" "Nobody's on Facebook anymore." Both takes are wrong, and they leave money on the table.

Facebook still has nearly 3 billion monthly users. The platform skews older than TikTok or Instagram, but older users buy more — and that's exactly why Facebook can be a quietly profitable place to monetize. This guide walks through what's actually working in 2026, what creators are really earning, and the methods most creator advice ignores.

Why Facebook Is Underrated for Creators

Most creator advice focuses on the trendy platforms because they're where creators want to be. But "where creators want to be" and "where the money is" aren't always the same place.

Facebook's audience trends older, more affluent, and more likely to actually click and buy. They join Groups. They watch Reels. They click through to websites instead of just scrolling. Engagement on Facebook isn't flashy, but the conversion rates often outperform other platforms because the audience has fewer distractions and more buying intent.

The platform also has the most mature monetization infrastructure of any social network. In-stream ads, Reels Play bonuses, paid online events, fan subscriptions, brand collaborations through the Brand Collabs Manager — Facebook has paid creators in some form longer than any of its competitors. The systems work, even if they're less talked about.

What Facebook Creators Actually Earn

Income on Facebook varies by niche even more than other platforms. A page in a profitable niche (finance, real estate, B2B services, parenting, health) with 50,000 followers can earn far more than a general meme page with 500,000. Some realistic income shapes:

  • Side income ($100-$1,000/month) — most monetizing creators sit here, combining a few small income streams.
  • Part-time income ($1,000-$5,000/month) — typical for creators who run an active Page or large Group with their own products.
  • Full-time income ($5,000-$20,000+/month) — usually creators who've built a real business: courses, memberships, services, or significant brand partnerships.

As with all platforms, the loudest income claims come from the top 1%. Most monetizing Facebook creators earn modest, useful income — which is still a great outcome.

Method 1: In-Stream Ads on Long-Form Videos

If you create longer videos, Facebook's in-stream ads (mid-roll, pre-roll, or image ads during videos) are one of the most reliable income streams the platform offers. Once you're eligible, Facebook automatically inserts ads into your videos and you earn a share of the revenue.

Eligibility requirements (these change, so check current Meta documentation):

  • Page or profile that meets Meta's monetization policies
  • Located in a country where the program is available
  • Minimum follower threshold (varies)
  • Minimum video performance (typically thousands of recent video views from videos at least 3 minutes long)
  • 60,000+ minutes watched in the past 60 days (this changes)

Realistic earnings: in-stream ad rates on Facebook are generally higher than YouTube's lowest-paying niches but lower than YouTube's highest. CPMs typically range from $1-$10 per thousand views, depending on niche and audience. A page getting one million video minutes per month might earn anywhere from a few hundred to a few thousand dollars.

Method 2: Facebook Reels Play Bonuses and Performance Programs

Facebook has run several short-form video bonus programs over the years, paying creators based on Reels performance. These programs come and go — sometimes invitation-only, sometimes open. The exact program available in your country and account in 2026 will depend on Meta's current rollouts.

The key truth about these programs: they're a bonus, not a business. Even when generous, payouts cap out at modest monthly amounts. Treat them as a "free money" layer on top of your real monetization, not the foundation. Programs disappear or change without much notice.

Method 3: Brand Partnerships via Brand Collabs Manager

Meta runs the Brand Collabs Manager — a marketplace where brands find Facebook (and Instagram) creators for paid partnerships. Listing your Page makes you discoverable to brands looking to run sponsored content. Many of the same brand deals that happen on Instagram also flow through Facebook, especially for creators with mature audiences.

Sponsored content rates on Facebook generally mirror Instagram pricing in similar niches. A creator with 50,000 engaged Facebook followers in a profitable niche can charge $500-$3,000 per branded post, with custom video integrations going higher.

Tips for landing Facebook brand deals:

  • Have a clear niche. Brands need to know who your audience is. A messy "lifestyle" Page is harder to sell than a focused "first-time parent budgeting" Page.
  • Show engagement, not just reach. Comments and shares matter more than passive views.
  • Pitch directly. Most deals still happen by emailing brands, not waiting in a marketplace.
  • Get contracts in writing. Verbal deals or chat-based agreements get messy fast. Especially watch for usage rights — if a brand wants to run your post as a paid ad, that's worth significantly more.

Method 4: Facebook Groups (One of the Most Underrated)

This is where serious creator businesses are quietly being built on Facebook. Groups create real community, drive ridiculous engagement, and put you in a stronger position than any Page.

You can monetize a Facebook Group in several ways:

  • Paid memberships. Facebook supports Subscription Groups in some regions, where members pay monthly to access exclusive content. You can also run paid groups outside Facebook's native subscription system using a separate payment platform.
  • Selling your own products to the Group. A free Group full of your ideal customers is the highest-converting audience you'll ever have. Course launches, coaching offers, and digital product sales all perform well to a warm Group.
  • Affiliate offers. Recommending tools or products you actually use, with affiliate tracking.
  • Sponsored Group posts. Less common, but some brands pay creators to feature them in active Groups (always disclose properly).

The most successful creator Groups have something in common: they solve a real problem for their members. Generic "lifestyle" or "creator support" Groups struggle. Tight-niche Groups ("Etsy sellers under $50K", "homeschooling moms in California", "freelance designers pricing services") thrive.

A 2,000-person Facebook Group full of your ideal customers is worth more than a 200,000-follower Page that doesn't convert.

Method 5: Online Courses and Digital Products

This is the highest-margin monetization path on any platform, and it works especially well with Facebook's older, more affluent audience. People who buy your course aren't paying for information — they're paying for transformation, structure, and the time you save them by organizing it all.

Common course pricing:

  • $50-$150 — beginner courses or short focused programs
  • $200-$500 — comprehensive multi-module courses
  • $500-$2,000 — premium courses with cohort components, live sessions, or accountability
  • $2,000+ — high-ticket programs, often with coaching included

Where Facebook helps: traffic. A Reel that hits 100,000 views can drive serious clicks to your course landing page. A Group launch can sell out a cohort in days. Live videos can run live sales sessions. Facebook's audience is conditioned to click and buy — they came up online during the era when you could click an ad and end up at a checkout page.

Method 6: 1:1 Coaching and Services

Facebook is also strong for selling your time. Coaching, consulting, audits, done-for-you services — these convert well on Facebook because the audience tends to make slower, considered decisions.

Service pricing varies enormously by niche, but service-based offers from Facebook usually fall in the $50-$500/hour range for coaching, with packages going significantly higher. A business consultant might charge $5,000 for a strategic engagement. A nutritionist might charge $300/month for ongoing client support.

The challenge with services is the operational lift: scheduling, payments, follow-ups, contracts, reschedule requests. The actual coaching is the easy part. The admin work eats your week. Tools that automate booking and payment flow are essentially recovering hours of your life every month.

Method 7: Affiliate Marketing

Facebook is one of the better platforms for affiliate marketing because:

  • Posts and Group threads can include links
  • The audience is older and more comfortable clicking links
  • Long-form content (long captions, longer videos) lets you actually explain why a product matters
  • Group conversations create natural recommendation moments

Niches where Facebook affiliate marketing tends to work especially well: home and garden, parenting, health and supplements, financial services, software and SaaS tools, hobbies (knitting, woodworking, photography). Almost anything where the audience is researching purchases.

Method 8: Paid Online Events

Facebook supports paid online events natively in many countries. You can charge admission to a livestream workshop, masterclass, or webinar. Attendees pay through Facebook, attend live, and get whatever value you're delivering.

This works well as a one-time campaign — host a paid 90-minute workshop on a specific topic, charge $19-$49, and invite your audience. It's a low-pressure way to test whether people will pay you for your expertise before building a full course.

Method 9: Digital Storefronts and Marketplace Selling

Facebook Marketplace is mostly for local physical goods, but Page-based selling and shop integrations let creators sell directly to their followers. If you have physical merch, print-on-demand, or your own e-commerce store, Facebook can drive real sales — especially if you've built a Group or Page audience that already trusts you.

What a Realistic Income Mix Looks Like

Most full-time Facebook creators run a combination of these. A common stack for someone earning $5,000-$15,000/month:

  • 40-60% from their own products (courses, memberships, coaching)
  • 15-25% from brand partnerships
  • 10-20% from affiliate marketing
  • 5-15% from in-stream ads, Reels bonuses, and other native programs
  • 5-10% from Group memberships or paid events

The exact mix depends on whether you have a Page, a Group, or both, and what niche you're in. The pattern is the same as every other platform: don't bet your entire income on one channel.

The Honest Take

Facebook isn't dead, isn't trendy, and isn't going anywhere. The platform pays creators in real, working systems and connects you with an audience that's older, has more disposable income, and is more likely to click an offer than scroll past it.

What it doesn't have is hype. You won't blow up overnight on Facebook the way you might on TikTok. You'll grow slower, but you'll grow more durably. The Group you build today will still be paying you in three years.

If you've been ignoring Facebook because it doesn't feel exciting, that's exactly the opportunity. The creators paying attention to it are quietly building real businesses while everyone else fights for attention on louder platforms.
