How to Find Your First 100 Paying Fans

Johnny L 10 min read

In 2008, Kevin Kelly wrote an essay called "1,000 True Fans." The argument was simple: a creator doesn't need millions of fans to make a living. They need 1,000 true ones, each willing to spend roughly $100 a year on whatever the creator makes. A musician, a writer, an artist, a podcaster (a category that barely existed when the essay was written) could build a real career on a thousand devoted people.

The essay aged remarkably well. It also got slightly weaponized. Every creator-economy article from 2015 onward cited it as if 1,000 true fans was the first milestone, the starting point, the obvious early target. Reality is messier. Most creators don't get to 1,000 paying fans by going for 1,000 paying fans. They get there by figuring out the first 100, then doubling, then doubling again.

This is the post about the first 100. Not the dream version where you launch and 1,000 people show up. The grinding, often-unglamorous version where the first 10 paying customers teach you everything you need to know about the next 90.

Why 100 First, Not 1,000

The "1,000 true fans" framing has a real problem: 1,000 is a number you can't meaningfully reason about when you have zero. It's too abstract. It feels like a destination you can't navigate to because you can't see it from where you're standing.

100 is different. 100 is a number you can roughly picture. It's a small wedding. It's a high school classroom four times over. It's the people who would show up to a launch party if you threw one. It's a number you can email or message individually if you needed to. And it's enough customers to teach you what they actually want, which is the only knowledge that scales the next 900.

The other reason 100 matters: 100 paying customers at $20-50 a month is real income. $2,000-5,000 monthly recurring revenue is enough to take the work seriously, justify the time, and reinvest in growth. It's the difference between a hobby that occasionally earns money and a business that needs to be run. It's also the milestone where most creators stop wondering "is this real?" and start asking "how do I grow this?"

100 is a number you can email individually. 1,000 is a number you can only market to. The first 100 teach you what the next 900 need.

The Difference Between a Follower and a Paying Fan

This is the part that catches creators off guard when they start trying to monetize. Followers and paying fans are different audiences. Sometimes wildly different.

A follower will like, comment, save, and share. They give you their attention for free. A paying fan will hand you actual money and trust you to deliver something in return. The conversion rate between the two is usually much lower than creators expect.

Industry benchmarks for creator monetization vary, but rough numbers look something like:

  • 0.5-2% of an Instagram or TikTok audience will buy a digital product when you launch it, if you've built any kind of relationship at all
  • 1-5% of an email list will buy from a launch email, depending on how warm the list is
  • 3-10% of a paid newsletter audience already considers themselves paying fans by definition
  • 5-15% of close-tier members (Patreon paid tiers, premium Discord, etc.) will buy additional products

If your follower count and engagement (something we explored in how to spot fake Instagram followers) are genuine, you can roughly estimate how big an audience needs to be to convert into 100 paying fans. For most creators it's somewhere between 5,000 and 30,000 followers (or 1,000-5,000 email subscribers) depending on niche and offer. Bigger audiences with weak engagement often convert worse than smaller audiences with strong engagement.

The First 10 Tell You Everything

Here's the part nobody emphasizes enough. The first 10 paying customers are not just income. They are the most valuable research data you will ever get. They show you:

  • Who actually pays you, not who you think will. You'll often be wrong about your buyer demographic. The fitness creator who assumed her audience was 20-somethings might find that her best-converting customers are 40-something professionals using fitness as stress relief.
  • What price point they accept without flinching. If 10 people bought at $19 without negotiation, $19 was too low. If 100 people bounced at $97, $97 was too high (or the offer was wrong).
  • Which platform brought them in. Track every customer back to where they first found you. You'll usually discover that one platform converts disproportionately better than the others, even if it has fewer followers.
  • What they actually wanted. Email those 10 customers after they buy. Ask what they hoped to get and what almost made them not buy. The objections you didn't know existed are the ones blocking customers 11 through 100.
  • What you can deliver consistently. Your first 10 customers will surface every operational weakness in your offer. The course where lesson 6 doesn't quite work. The coaching package that takes 3x the time you priced for. The digital product with a confusing onboarding email. Fix everything before you push for the next 90.

Most creators rush through the first 10 because the income is small and the affirmation feels insufficient. That's exactly backwards. The first 10 are the most valuable customers you'll ever have. Treat them like co-creators of your business, not statistical noise.

The Monetization Ladder

Different paying fans pay different amounts. Most creators who reach 100 paying fans don't get there by selling one thing to 100 people. They get there with a ladder of products at different price points, where the same fan often climbs from low to high as trust grows.

A useful mental model has four rungs:

Rung 1: Free with intent

This isn't really a "rung," but it's the foundation. Free content (Instagram posts, TikTok videos, YouTube videos, blog posts) where the goal is to lead the audience to give you their email address or follow you somewhere durable. Free content alone doesn't pay rent. Free content that captures contact information does, eventually.

Rung 2: Low-ticket digital products ($10-50)

Templates, presets, ebooks, mini-courses, Notion templates, lightroom presets, recipe packs, workout guides. Low-friction purchases that let someone try paying you without thinking too hard about it. A digital products tool makes this practical even when you only have one or two things to sell. The first $19 someone gives you is wildly more meaningful than the first $1,000.

Rung 3: Mid-ticket courses or memberships ($50-500)

A real course, a paid newsletter, a Patreon tier, a Discord community, a recurring coaching program. This is where most creators find their primary income engine. The customer has bought from you before (or thoroughly trusted your free content), is willing to commit to a larger purchase, and wants ongoing value. Recurring memberships at $5-20/month, courses at $97-297, and small group programs at $300-500 all live here.

Rung 4: High-ticket services ($500-5,000+)

One-on-one coaching, done-for-you services, intensive cohorts, premium consulting. Fewer customers, much higher revenue per customer. Not for everyone (the work delivery model is different), but for creators who can deliver real outcomes, this is where the math gets interesting. 1:1 booking with integrated payment makes this a natural ladder rung once trust is built.

Most creators with 100 paying fans have customers spread across rungs 2, 3, and sometimes 4. Pure single-product businesses are rare in the creator economy and usually a sign that someone is leaving money on the table. The same audience that bought your $19 template will often happily buy your $97 course later.

Where the First 100 Actually Come From

If you talk to creators who passed 100 paying fans, the source mix is consistent enough to notice. Most look something like this:

  • The first 5-15 come from your existing personal network. Friends, family, professional contacts, people who watched you build the thing and want to support it. This sounds embarrassing but every successful creator business started here.
  • The next 20-40 come from your warmest social audience: the people who DM you, leave thoughtful comments, share your work unprompted. These are the people you'd recognize if you saw their handle.
  • The next 30-50 come from your email list (if you have one, and you should) and from one or two pieces of content that disproportionately drove buyers. Often a single post or video that struck a nerve and resonated for weeks or months after publication.
  • The last 10-30 come from word-of-mouth among existing buyers. Customer 50 brings customer 51 because they posted about their purchase or recommended it in a group chat.

The unsexy truth: very few creators get their first 100 paying fans from a viral moment. They get them from a slower, more compounding process of warm audience plus consistent content plus a clearly priced offer. The viral moment, if it comes, multiplies what's already working. It doesn't create it from nothing.

The Boring Things That Matter

Some operational details that disproportionately affect whether someone actually buys:

A real link in bio

Your audience needs a frictionless way to get from "interested" to "checkout." A bio link with three clearly labeled options (free thing, paid thing, way to reach you) outperforms a tangle of seven different platforms every time. What to put in your link in bio covers the structure that converts.

An owned audience, not just a social one

Email is unsexy and still the highest-converting channel in the creator economy. A 1,000-person email list often outperforms a 50,000-follower Instagram for actual sales. Starting a newsletter as a parallel track to your social content is one of the highest-leverage moves a creator can make before launching paid offers.

Clarity about what they're buying

Most creator landing pages are vague. "Transform your life" doesn't sell. "A 4-week course with weekly video lessons, a workbook, and a private Discord, designed for new graphic designers building their first portfolio" sells. Specificity beats inspiration. Tell people exactly what they get, when they get it, and what changes after.

A way to receive payment without losing 10-30% to fees

This is where platform fees from third-party tools quietly eat into creator income. A creator selling a $19 product on a platform that takes 15% loses almost $3 per sale. Across 100 sales, that's $285 you could have kept. (Ryxa takes 0% transaction fees specifically because platform fees eating creator income is the single biggest tax on solo creators.) Stripe's processing fee still applies, but the platform layer above it shouldn't.

The single biggest difference between creators who reach 100 paying fans in six months and creators who never reach it is often just clarity. Clear offer, clear price, clear delivery, clear next step. Vague offers don't convert no matter how big the audience is.

Common Mistakes That Block Progress

These show up in nearly every creator who plateaus at 5-15 paying fans:

  • Waiting for the audience to be "ready." Most creators wait too long to launch their first paid offer. They wait for 10K followers, then 50K, then 100K. The audience was usually ready 30K followers ago. Launch the first thing earlier than you feel comfortable. The data from your first 10 customers is more valuable than the next 10K followers.
  • Pricing for who they were six months ago. The pricing instinct of "I would have paid $9 for this" is almost always wrong. Most creators undercharge by 2-3x on their first offer. (We covered the dynamics of this in how to price your first online course and how much should creators charge for coaching.)
  • Selling to followers instead of customers. A follower is a fan of your free content. A customer has a problem your paid offer solves. These are different framings and the second one converts. The question "what problem am I solving" is more useful than "what am I selling."
  • Treating launches as one-time events. A first launch is rarely a permanent income stream. Customers come from continuous launches, evergreen funnels, and the second and third products that solve adjacent problems for the same audience.
  • Focusing on the algorithm instead of the audience. A creator obsessed with whether their reach is up or down today is usually not the same creator who's emailing customer 47 to ask how the course is going. The latter is the one who reaches 100 paying fans.

The 100-to-1,000 Bridge

Once you have 100 paying fans, the path to 1,000 looks different. The first 100 came from manual outreach, network effects, and getting the offer right. The next 900 come from systems: an evergreen funnel that converts cold traffic, content that ranks in search or recommendation, a referral structure where existing customers bring new ones, and possibly paid ads if the unit economics work.

That's a different post. The first 100 is its own milestone with its own playbook, and a lot of creators never write that second post for themselves because they got stuck somewhere between 10 and 30 and gave up. The good news: the dynamics of getting from 10 to 100 are completely learnable. The bad news: there's no shortcut around the patience part.

The Realistic Bottom Line

You don't need a million followers. You don't even need 100,000. You need a clear offer, a small but engaged audience, an owned channel (email, even if it's tiny), a way to take payment that doesn't tax you on every sale, and the patience to treat your first 10 paying customers like the most valuable people in your business. Because they are.

100 paying fans isn't the end of the journey. It's the point at which the journey becomes navigable. The first 10 prove the offer. The first 100 prove the business. Everything after is iteration on what already works.

Start the offer. Tell the warmest part of your audience. Send the first email. Take the first $19. Pay attention to who hands it to you. The next 99 are easier than the first one.
