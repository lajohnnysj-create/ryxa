💰 Creator Pricing

How Much Should Creators Charge for 1:1 Coaching?

Ryxa 📅 May 2026 ⏱ 10 min read

You've been getting DMs from followers asking how you do what you do. A few have hinted they'd pay for your time. You're sitting on a real opportunity — one-on-one coaching is one of the highest-margin things a creator can offer — but you're stuck on one question: what's the right price?

Charge too little and you devalue the work, attract the wrong students, and burn yourself out trading hours for pennies. Charge too much and nobody books, and you sit there wondering if anyone takes your expertise seriously.

This is a practical pricing guide based on what creators are actually charging in 2026 — by tier, by audience size, by niche. No vague "charge what you're worth" filler. Just numbers and the reasoning behind them.

The Three-Tier Framework

Most creator coaching falls into one of three pricing tiers, and where you land depends less on your follower count than people assume. It depends on three things:

  • Proof. Can you point to specific results — your own or your past clients'?
  • Niche specificity. Generic "creator advice" commands less than "how to grow a fitness Instagram from 0 to 50K"
  • Time delivered. A 30-minute call is priced very differently from a 60-minute call with a follow-up document.

Use these as your floor and ceiling. Adjust based on the criteria above.

Tier 1: Starter

$50 – $150 per session

Who this is for: Creators under 10K followers, or creators of any size testing coaching for the first time. You have real expertise but limited public proof of teaching outcomes.

What you typically deliver: A 30-45 minute call. Maybe a brief follow-up note with three takeaways. No multi-session packages yet.

Why this range works: Low enough that followers will book on impulse, high enough that you're not undervaluing the work. Most creators massively underprice at this stage — $25 sessions attract people who don't take the call seriously, and you'll resent the work within ten clients.

Tier 2: Established

$150 – $400 per session

Who this is for: Creators with a clear niche, 10K-100K followers, and a track record. You can point to specific outcomes — "I grew this account from X to Y" or "I helped these three clients launch their first product."

What you typically deliver: 60-minute call. A pre-call questionnaire so you arrive prepared. A summary doc with action items. Often sold as a 3-session or 5-session package at a small discount.

Why this range works: You're charging for outcomes, not just time. The followers willing to pay this aren't impulse-buyers — they're investing in a result. Your conversion rate goes down compared to Tier 1, but the people who do book are committed and easier to help.

Tier 3: Premium

$400 – $1,500+ per session

Who this is for: Creators with substantial proof — six-figure income, mid-six-figure follower counts, or a documented coaching track record with named past clients. You're not selling expertise — you're selling access to a top performer.

What you typically deliver: 60-90 minute call. Detailed pre-work. Comprehensive follow-up document. Often only sold in packages ($2,000-$5,000+ for 4-8 sessions). Sometimes includes async support via email or Voxer between sessions.

Why this range works: At this tier, the price is partly the filter. People paying $1,000 for a session show up prepared and treat your time accordingly. The work feels different — and you can sustain doing it because each booking is meaningful revenue, not a grind.

The single biggest pricing mistake creators make is starting too low. The second biggest is staying there.

What Audience Size Actually Tells You

Follower count is a weak predictor of fair coaching price, but it's not nothing. Here's a more honest read:

  • Under 10K: Stick to Tier 1 unless you have unusual specific expertise (years of professional experience, certifications, documented client results outside of social media). Most creators here should start at $75-$100 and work up.
  • 10K-50K: Mid-Tier 1 to early Tier 2. $100-$200 per session is the sweet spot for most niches.
  • 50K-250K: Solidly Tier 2. $200-$350 per session, with package pricing being more common than single sessions.
  • 250K-1M: Late Tier 2 to early Tier 3. $300-$600 per session, often with package-only offerings.
  • 1M+: Tier 3 and above. At this scale, single-session bookings are often discontinued in favor of high-ticket programs ($3,000-$10,000+) or curated mastermind groups.

Two important caveats:

First, niche matters enormously. A 5K-follower creator with deep expertise in a high-paying niche (B2B marketing, fitness for executives, financial planning for freelancers) can often charge Tier 2 prices because the audience has budget. A 500K-follower lifestyle account where most followers are teenagers may struggle to charge above Tier 1 because the audience can't pay.

Second, niche specificity matters more than niche size. "Fitness coaching" is generic. "Fitness coaching for postpartum moms returning to running" is specific. The specific version commands higher prices because the people who book have narrower problems and clearer expectations of value.

How to Decide Where You Land

If you're stuck between tiers, this five-question test usually clarifies things:

  • Can you point to a specific outcome you achieved that's directly relevant to what you'd coach? If yes, you're at least Tier 2.
  • Have you successfully helped at least three other people get a similar result? If yes, you're solidly Tier 2 or higher.
  • Is your niche one where the audience typically has budget? If no, stay in lower-Tier 1 to mid-Tier 2 territory regardless of expertise.
  • Are you uncomfortable charging the price? If yes, you're probably charging the right amount. Discomfort is normal at every price point.
  • Would charging this price filter out tire-kickers and attract serious clients? If yes, that's a feature. The wrong clients are more expensive than the right ones at any price.

The Mistakes Creators Make Most Often

Underpricing to "be nice"

You discount because you don't want to seem out of reach to your audience. The result: you attract people who treat the call casually, miss appointments, ask for refunds over minor issues, and don't implement the advice. Then you burn out and quit coaching.

Premium pricing is partly a self-protection mechanism. Charging fairly is how you stay in the game long enough to keep helping people.

Pricing in a vacuum

Don't set your rate based on what feels right in your head. Look at what other creators in your specific niche charge — most have public coaching pages. Look at adjacent industries (consultants, therapists, business coaches in your niche). You're competing for the same dollars, and your pricing should reflect that landscape, not just your follower count.

Not raising prices once demand exists

If you're consistently booked, your rate is too low. Demand-based pricing is how every other professional service works, and creator coaching is no different. Raise rates 25-40% every six months until you see resistance — that's how you find the actual ceiling.

Quick math on raising rates: If you go from $150 to $250 per session and your booking rate drops by 30%, you're still earning roughly the same per month with fewer hours and less burnout. If you go from $250 to $400 and lose 50% of bookings, you're earning 20% more for half the work. Most creators discover the higher number is genuinely more sustainable.

Hiding the price

"DM for rates" is a friction point that costs you more than transparent pricing ever would. Serious clients want to know upfront. Window-shoppers waste your DMs asking, then disappearing. Just publish the number. Yes, even if you think it's too high.

The Practical Setup

Once you've landed on a price, the operational side matters as much as the price itself. Here's what's worth getting right from session one:

  • Take payment upfront. Always. No exceptions. People who object to upfront payment are a yellow flag.
  • Have a clear cancellation policy. 24 or 48-hour notice for free reschedule. Inside that window, the session is forfeit. This protects your time and signals professionalism.
  • Use a real booking system. Don't coordinate via DM. A proper system (with calendar sync, payment, automated reminders) reduces no-shows by half and frees your brain.
  • Send a pre-session questionnaire. Three or four questions about what they want to walk away with. Pre-work makes the call substantially better and signals you take it seriously.
  • Follow up with a written summary. Even a few bullet points of "what we covered and what you committed to." Massively improves the perceived value of the call.

The systems aren't the work — the coaching is. But the systems make the work sustainable, and they're often the difference between coaching being a delight and coaching being a logistical nightmare.

The Bottom Line

Pick a number that's slightly higher than feels comfortable. Run with it for ten clients. Then raise it. Repeat.

The creators who build sustainable coaching businesses aren't the ones who guessed the perfect price on day one. They're the ones who started, gathered data from real clients, and adjusted. The first price you set isn't the price you'll charge in a year — it's a starting point.

Don't overthink it. Don't let pricing paralysis stop you from launching. Set the rate, publish the page, and go.
