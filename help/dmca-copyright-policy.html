DMCA Copyright Policy

Ryxa respects the intellectual property rights of others and complies with the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA), the U.S. federal law that governs how online platforms handle copyright infringement claims.

If you believe content on Ryxa infringes a copyright you own, this page explains how to submit a formal DMCA takedown notice, what happens after we receive it, and how someone can respond if they believe their content was removed in error.

How to submit a DMCA takedown notice

To be effective under the DMCA, your notice must include specific information required by law. Please include all of the following when you contact us:

  1. A description of the copyrighted work you believe has been infringed (for example, the title, registration number if any, or a link to where you originally published it).
  2. A specific link or URL to the Ryxa page or content that is allegedly infringing, so we can find it.
  3. Your full legal name, mailing address, telephone number, and email address.
  4. A statement that you have a good-faith belief that the use of the material is not authorized by the copyright owner, its agent, or the law.
  5. A statement, made under penalty of perjury, that the information in your notice is accurate and that you are the copyright owner or are authorized to act on the copyright owner's behalf.
  6. Your physical or electronic signature.

Submitting a false DMCA notice has consequences.

Under Section 512(f) of the DMCA, a person who knowingly materially misrepresents that content is infringing may be liable for damages, including costs and attorneys' fees, suffered by the alleged infringer. Please only submit a notice if you actually own (or are authorized to act for) the copyright in question.

What happens after you submit a notice

Once we receive a valid DMCA notice, we typically:

  • Review the notice to confirm it includes the required information above.
  • Remove or disable access to the allegedly infringing content within 10 business days.
  • Notify the user whose content was removed and provide them with a copy of the notice.
  • Inform them of their right to file a counter-notice (see below).

If a notice is incomplete or missing required information, we may ask for clarification before acting on it.

If your content was removed and you believe that was wrong

If your content was removed because of a DMCA notice and you believe the removal was a mistake (for example, you own the rights, you have permission to use the work, or your use is fair use), you can file a DMCA counter-notice. To be effective, your counter-notice must include:

  1. Identification of the content that was removed and the location (URL) where it appeared on Ryxa before removal.
  2. A statement, made under penalty of perjury, that you have a good-faith belief that the content was removed as a result of mistake or misidentification.
  3. Your full legal name, mailing address, telephone number, and email address.
  4. A statement that you consent to the jurisdiction of the federal court in the district where you live (or, if outside the United States, the federal court in the Central District of California), and that you will accept service of process from the person who filed the original notice.
  5. Your physical or electronic signature.

If we receive a valid counter-notice, we will forward it to the original complaining party. Under the DMCA, we may restore the removed content within 10 to 14 business days unless the original complainant notifies us that they have filed a lawsuit seeking a court order to keep the content offline.

Filing a false counter-notice also has consequences.

Section 512(f) of the DMCA imposes liability for knowingly materially misrepresenting that content was removed by mistake. Only file a counter-notice if you genuinely believe the original notice was incorrect.

Repeat infringers

In accordance with the DMCA, Ryxa terminates the accounts of users who are determined to be repeat infringers. Receiving multiple valid DMCA notices may result in your account being suspended or removed from Ryxa.

If your situation is not a DMCA matter

The DMCA process is specifically for U.S. copyright claims. If your concern is about a trademark, or about something that does not clearly involve copyright, see our Intellectual Property Infringement article for the more general reporting path. Both processes lead to a real person on our team, but the DMCA process gives you the formal legal protections of Section 512 of the DMCA.

A note on legal advice

The DMCA is a U.S. federal law with specific requirements and procedures. This article explains how we handle DMCA notices on Ryxa, but it is not legal advice. If you are unsure whether your situation calls for a DMCA notice or whether you have a valid copyright claim, we recommend consulting an attorney.
