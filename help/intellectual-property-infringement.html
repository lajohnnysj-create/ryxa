Intellectual Property Infringement

Ryxa gives creators a place to share their own work and build their own brand. We take that seriously, which means we also take intellectual property seriously. If someone uses your copyrighted work or your trademark on Ryxa without permission, you can report it to us and we will review it.

This article explains what counts as an intellectual property issue, how to report one, and what to do if you believe action was taken on your account by mistake.

What counts as infringement

Intellectual property infringement on Ryxa generally falls into two categories.

Copyright

Copyright protects original creative work, such as photos, videos, written content, music, artwork, and design. A copyright issue on Ryxa might be an avatar or background image that is your photo or artwork, or a link leading to content that copies your work, used without your permission.

Trademark

A trademark protects a brand name, logo, or similar mark that identifies a business. A trademark issue on Ryxa might be a username, display name, or logo that uses your registered trademark in a way that could confuse people about who is behind the page.

Infringement can show up in different places on a Ryxa page, including the username, the profile name, an avatar or background image, or content reached through a link on the page.

How to report an infringement

If you have found a Ryxa page that infringes your copyright or trademark, email us at hello@ryxa.io. Our team will review every report. Please include as much of the following as you can, because a clear report is much faster to act on.

For any report, include:

The link to the Ryxa page involved, a description of exactly what on that page is infringing, and your contact details so we can follow up.

If your report is about copyright

Describe the specific work being infringed and explain how the Ryxa page uses it without permission. Include proof that you own the work, for example a link to where you originally published it, or another record showing it is yours.

If your report is about a trademark

Provide official documentation that shows you own the trademark. This usually means a registration certificate from a trademark office. Please note that a screenshot of a public search result, such as a listing on a trademark office website, is not on its own accepted as proof of ownership. We need documentation that actually establishes the registration is yours.

Submitting a report in good faith matters. Reports should be genuine claims about your own rights, not a way to target a creator you simply disagree with.

What happens after you report

Once we receive a report, we review the page and the claim. If we find that content or an account violates intellectual property rights, we may remove the content or take action on the account, consistent with our Community Standards and Terms of Service. We will use the contact details you provided to share updates or ask for anything further we need.

We cannot promise a specific outcome or timeline, but every report sent to us is read and reviewed by a person.

If you believe action was taken in error

If your content was removed or your account was actioned because of an intellectual property report, and you believe that was a mistake, you can dispute it. This might be the case if you actually have permission to use the work, if you own the rights yourself, or if you believe the report was not valid.

To dispute it, email us at hello@ryxa.io. Tell us which account or content is involved, that you are disputing an intellectual property action, and explain clearly why you believe the action should be reviewed. Include any documentation that supports your position, such as proof that you own the work or have permission to use it.

If your account has been suspended, you can also read My Account Got Banned for more on how account reviews and appeals work.

A note on what we can and cannot decide

Ryxa reviews reports and acts on its own platform, but we are not a court and we cannot resolve a legal dispute between two parties. Intellectual property law is complex, and serious disputes may need legal advice. If a matter is significant, we may recommend that the people involved seek their own legal counsel.
